\begin{abstract}
Generative AI is transforming academic publishing by dramatically reducing the cost of manuscript production while review capacity grows far more slowly.
Submission volumes have surged to record levels, a rising share of peer reviews contain AI-generated text, and verification infrastructure has not kept pace.
We study this asymmetry---the fact that AI scales paper production faster than it scales screening---using a game-theoretic model in which AI adoption raises submission volume and lowers drafting cost faster than it raises review capacity, while also increasing the rate at which low-quality manuscripts pass screening.
We find three results.
First, extreme slot scarcity can stabilize honest submission norms even under degraded screening, because the publication lottery becomes too hard to win.
Second, gradual AI adoption can trigger a discrete tipping point into a low-quality long-run outcome; once tipped, the system is self-sustaining, so recovery requires a large coordinated intervention, not incremental reform.
Third, expanding publication capacity without scaling verification accelerates the tipping.
These results point to a concrete policy agenda of verification-centric institutions (structured audits, submission quotas, and decoupled certification) rather than simply more publication slots.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

We ask what happens to a publishing \emph{system} when it becomes substantially cheaper and faster to produce a manuscript than to review one.
By September 2024, more than one-fifth of computer-science preprints on arXiv showed measurable evidence of large-language-model modification \citep{Liang2025}, and at least 13.5\% of all biomedical abstracts published that year contained AI-assisted text \citep{Kobak2025}.
Monthly submissions to arXiv reached a record 24{,}226 in October 2024, roughly 50\% higher than in late 2022 \citep{arXiv2024}.
Verification has not kept pace.
The rate at which researchers accept reviewer invitations has declined steadily over two decades \citep{Meyerson2025}, and between 6.5\% and 16.9\% of conference peer reviews now contain substantial AI-generated text \citep{Liang2024ICML}.
More than half of active researchers surveyed in 2025 reported using AI tools to write reviews, often against explicit journal policy \citep{Naddaf2025}.
The question is not whether individual researchers behave ethically, but whether the system as a whole can absorb an asymmetric shock to production and verification.

While the conventional response to AI in science focuses on disclosure requirements, detection tools, and editorial guidelines, these measures address individual conduct and leave untouched a structural problem: even if every author is honest, reviewers can scrutinize only a finite number of manuscripts carefully per cycle.
When submission volume rises faster than review capacity, screening degrades---not because of misconduct, but because of congestion.
A reviewer who faces twice as many papers in the same time cannot give each submission the same attention.
Congested screening lowers the effective quality threshold, and a lower threshold raises the expected payoff from submitting weak or low-quality work.
We focus on this feedback between volume and screening quality as the central mechanism.

We distinguish two bottlenecks in the publishing system, and we argue that AI affects them asymmetrically.
The first is verification capacity: the number of papers that reviewers can evaluate carefully in a given period, denoted $K_{rev}$.
The second is allocation capacity: the number of papers the journal can publish per period, denoted $K$.
AI raises submission volume $V$ by reducing the marginal cost of drafting and resubmitting papers, lowering the effort cost $c$ of each submission.
It may also increase the false-positive rate $\lambda$---the probability that a low-quality manuscript passes peer review---because AI-assisted writing produces polished text that is harder to distinguish from genuine contributions on surface features alone.
Review capacity, by contrast, does not scale symmetrically.
Deep expertise, data and code verification, and replication checking remain human-intensive tasks, and journal policies in many fields explicitly restrict AI use in reviews \citep{Naddaf2025}.
Production, in short, scales faster with AI adoption than verification does.

We model this asymmetry in a game-theoretic framework, drawing on strategic models of scientific communication \citep{Zollman2024, Thurner2011}.
We consider a population of authors who choose between two strategies: submitting only genuine work (the cooperative strategy) or submitting everything including low-quality manuscripts (the defecting strategy).
A journal applies peer review, subject to both a review capacity $K_{rev}$ and a publication slot constraint $K$.
We introduce an AI adoption intensity parameter $a \in [0,1]$.
Higher $a$ raises submission volume $V$ with elasticity $\gamma$, raises review capacity $K_{rev}$ with elasticity $\delta < \gamma$, lowers submission cost $c$, and raises the false-positive rate $\lambda$.
The production elasticity exceeds the verification elasticity by assumption, formalizing the asymmetry observed empirically.
The net effect of $a$ on the profitability of submitting a low-quality paper, $\Pi_{bad}(x, a) = r \cdot \bar{\rho}(x,a) \cdot \pi_0(x,a) - c(a)$, is positive: AI makes spamming more attractive.

We find three results.
First, extreme slot scarcity can stabilize honest submission norms even under degraded screening, because the publication lottery becomes too hard to win.
Scarcity, paradoxically, acts as a deterrent---but AI gradually erodes this protection by lowering the submission cost $c(a)$, so the deterrent requires progressively tighter slot constraints as adoption deepens.
Second, gradual AI adoption can trigger a discrete tipping point into a low-quality long-run outcome; once tipped, the system is self-sustaining.
Recovery requires a large coordinated intervention, not incremental reform: the value of $a$ at which quality collapses is strictly lower than the value at which quality recovers, so the system exhibits hysteresis.
Third, expanding publication capacity (raising $K$) without scaling verification makes the situation worse, not better.
More slots raise the expected payoff from submitting marginal work; under AI adoption, where $c(a)$ is already low and $\lambda(a)$ already elevated, even a modest improvement in the publication lottery is enough to push the system across the tipping threshold.

These results point to a concrete policy agenda of verification-centric institutions---structured audits, submission quotas, and decoupled certification---rather than simply more publication slots.
Effective intervention must target the two primary levers: raising genuine review capacity $K_{rev}$ and reducing the false-positive rate $\lambda$.
These are not incremental reforms; they constitute a different institutional design, one oriented toward verification rather than volume.

\section{Stylized Facts: AI as an Asymmetric Shock}
\label{sec:stylized_facts}

\paragraph{We draw on evidence that AI dramatically lowers the cost of producing a manuscript.}
Large language models have cut the time required to draft, revise, and resubmit academic text by a documented margin.
In a randomised experiment with 453 college-educated professionals, \citet{NoyZhang2023} found that access to ChatGPT reduced the time to complete an incentivised writing task by approximately 40\% while simultaneously raising assessed output quality by 18 percentage points, with the largest gains accruing to weaker writers.
\citet{StokelWalker2023} report that researchers can revise a full manuscript in under five minutes at an API cost below \$0.50.
These micro-level reductions compound at the submission level: iterative resubmission across journals was once a significant labour cost, and AI converts that friction into a near-zero marginal activity, giving every author---regardless of the underlying quality of their work---a cost-effective reason to submit or resubmit.
In our model, this channel corresponds to a downward shift in the submission cost parameter $c(a)$.

\paragraph{Aggregate data confirm a sharp rise in submission pressure.}
Macro-level data are consistent with the micro-level cost story.
Monthly submissions to arXiv reached a record 24{,}226 in October 2024, roughly 50\% above the late-2022 baseline; the first month to cross 20{,}000 submissions was May 2023, shortly after the public release of ChatGPT \citep{arXiv2024}.
More than one-fifth of computer-science preprints sampled in September 2024 showed measurable evidence of large-language-model modification \citep{Liang2025}, and a vocabulary-based analysis of 15 million biomedical abstracts found that at least 13.5\% of all 2024 \textit{PubMed} papers contain AI-assisted text, with rates exceeding 40\% in some sub-disciplines \citep{Kobak2025}.
Alongside this volume growth, annual retraction counts exceeded 10{,}000 for the first time in 2023, with paper-mill activity implicated in mass retraction events at major publishers.
These data show that submission volume $V$ has shifted materially upward as AI adoption has spread, independently of any change in the fraction of authors who genuinely produce high-quality work.
In our model, this corresponds to an upward shift in $V(x, a)$ with elasticity $\gamma$ with respect to $a$.

\paragraph{While text production scales with AI, verification does not.}
Rigorous peer review requires reading carefully for logical consistency, checking data against claimed analyses, running or inspecting code, and evaluating genuine novelty---tasks that remain human-intensive in ways that text generation is not.
Confidentiality requirements for submitted manuscripts prevent reviewers from uploading papers to third-party AI services, and many journals explicitly forbid AI use in the review process \citep{Naddaf2025}.
Even where AI-assisted reviewing is nominally permitted, the marginal reviewer who can detect a subtle methodological flaw is a scarce specialist whose attention is not fungible with a language model's output.
Reviewer acceptance rates have declined continuously for two decades \citep{Meyerson2025}, and the existing burden is already extreme: reviewers contribute more than 100 million hours annually to the peer review system worldwide, with the load concentrated among a small fraction of active researchers \citep{Kovanis2016}.
We therefore expect that a system already under strain before the AI era cannot absorb a near-doubling of submission volume through a commensurate increase in review capacity.
In our model, this channel is captured by $K_{rev}(a) = K_{rev}(0) \cdot (1 + \delta a)$, where the elasticity $\delta$ is strictly smaller than the production elasticity $\gamma$, reflecting the structural and policy constraints described above.

\paragraph{AI also polishes manuscripts without improving their reliability, which may raise the false-positive rate.}
A third channel operates through the quality of the screening signal rather than through volume.
AI writing tools produce fluent, well-formatted, and stylistically consistent text \citep{StokelWalker2023}, and this surface polish reduces the ability of reviewers to distinguish a low-quality submission from a genuine contribution on textual features alone, raising the probability that a flawed manuscript passes the review filter.
The screening process degrades further when reviewers themselves turn to AI: between 6.5\% and 16.9\% of AI-conference peer reviews now contain substantial AI-generated text \citep{Liang2024ICML}, and an experimental audit found that AI-assisted reviews award systematically higher scores, leading to elevated acceptance rates \citep{Latona2024}.
Automated detection offers limited recourse---leading AI detectors exhibit false-negative rates above 70\%, and adversarial paraphrasing can reduce recall to near zero \citep{Liang2023detectors}.
In our model, this channel corresponds to an upward shift in the false-positive rate $\lambda(a)$: as AI adoption rises, the probability that a low-quality submission passes review increases even holding the underlying composition of the author population constant.

\paragraph{We summarize these channels as a single asymmetry hypothesis.}
The four channels described above---rising submission volume, falling drafting cost, lagging review capacity, and rising false-positive risk---reduce to a single structural claim:
\begin{quote}
  \textit{AI raises the elasticity of manuscript production above the elasticity of manuscript verification.}
\end{quote}
We formalise this as the \textbf{AI asymmetry assumption}: with $a \in [0,1]$ denoting AI adoption intensity,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:asymmetry}
    \gamma > \delta \geq 0,
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ is the elasticity of submission volume with respect to $a$ and $\delta$ is the elasticity of review capacity with respect to $a$.
This is not an axiom but a testable empirical statement about relative scaling rates.
Table~\ref{tab:ai_channels} translates each channel into a specific model parameter and states the direction of the shift.
In Section~\ref{sec:analysis}, we examine how our qualitative results depend on the magnitude of the gap $\gamma - \delta$ and on the slope of $\lambda(a)$.

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:literature}

We draw on research showing that peer review functions as a shared resource under increasing strain.
Each submission consumes a fixed amount of reviewer time and attention, and when submission volume grows faster than the pool of willing reviewers, that resource degrades.
\citet{gross2019contest} show that scientific contests become inefficient at high participation rates: total effort rises, but expected quality per participant falls.
\citet{Cotton2013} reaches a parallel conclusion for journals: a small entry cost can deter low-quality submissions and relieve pressure on reviewers.
\citet{Kovanis2016} estimate that the global peer review system already consumed over 100 million hours of researcher time each year in the mid-2010s, and \citet{Aczel2021} value the US contribution alone at more than 1.5 billion USD in 2020.
\citet{Bianchi2018} model these pressures and find that screening quality declines even when reviewers act in good faith, simply because competing demands leave too little time per paper.
The reviewing system, in short, was already near its limits before generative artificial intelligence entered the picture.

While congestion models treat reviewers as passive, a related body of work models authors as strategic agents---and this is where the feedback loop becomes visible.
\citet{Thurner2011} show that when the review filter is imprecise, submitting everything regardless of quality can become individually rational, pushing the long-run outcome toward low average quality.
\citet{Zollman2024} extend this to a multi-journal setting and find that prestige-maximising incentives can reward strategic over honest behaviour.
\citet{Tiokhin2021} study honest signalling and show that the good outcome---where only strong work is submitted---is fragile: it collapses when the cost of mimicking quality falls even slightly.
\citet{Smaldino2016} document this logic empirically, arguing that ``publish or perish'' incentives act as a selection force that spreads high-volume, low-quality strategies through academic communities.
At the aggregate level, \citet{Ioannidis2005} shows that most published findings fail to replicate, not because of fraud, but because structural incentives reward output over reproducibility.
\citet{casadevall2014reforming} and \citet{Stephan2012} trace this to career and grant structures that treat paper counts as a proxy for scientific value.
Paper mills---commercial services producing fabricated manuscripts to order---represent the extreme end of this logic: annual retraction counts have reached record highs and single incidents have implicated thousands of papers at major publishers.
Our model formalises the mechanism underlying these trends: when the review filter is weak and slots are available, submitting low-quality work yields a positive expected payoff, and selection pressure then favours volume over quality.

In contrast to the strategic submission literature, recent empirical work documents how generative artificial intelligence reshapes both sides of the system simultaneously.
On the production side, \citet{NoyZhang2023} find that access to a large language model cuts professional writing time by 40\% and raises rated output quality by 18 percentage points.
\citet{Liang2025} detect language-model modification in more than one-fifth of computer-science preprints by late 2024, and \citet{Kobak2025} find AI-assisted text in at least 13.5\% of biomedical abstracts from the same period.
On the evaluation side, however, the picture is more troubling.
\citet{Checco2021} conclude that deep verification---checking data, code, and logical consistency---remains beyond current automated systems.
\citet{Liang2024ICML} find that between 6.5\% and 16.9\% of reviews at major conferences already contain AI-generated text, and \citet{Latona2024} show experimentally that AI-assisted reviews award higher scores, raising acceptance rates.
\citet{Naddaf2025} reports that more than half of surveyed researchers now use AI tools for reviewing, often against journal policy.
These findings supply the empirical foundation for our asymmetry assumption: artificial intelligence raises submission productivity faster than it raises verification capacity.

A fourth strand addresses the economics of gatekeeping and institutional design.
\citet{Ellison2002} shows that the equilibrium publication standard depends on how strongly readers value quality signals.
\citet{MullerItten2022} finds that journals maintain strict standards to protect their certification value: accepting too freely devalues their own signal.
\citet{casadevall2014reforming} argue that effective reform must target the false-positive rate directly---through data deposition, structured reporting, and replication requirements---rather than adjusting slot counts alone.
\citet{Ginsparg2011} shows that dissemination and certification can be separated: preprint servers make manuscripts available immediately while editorial endorsement follows independently.
\citet{Cotton2013} provides the sharpest economic case for submission fees: a small cost of entry restores friction, filters marginal submissions, and reduces reviewer overload.

\subsection*{What we contribute}

While these literatures each address part of the problem, none unifies them.
Models of strategic submission do not treat artificial intelligence as a structural shock that shifts multiple inputs at once.
The empirical AI-in-science literature documents faster production, slower review growth, and higher false-positive risk, but does not connect these to a model with clear predictions about the long-run state of the system.
Neither strand addresses the possibility that submission pressure can grow gradually until it crosses a threshold, after which the system shifts suddenly to a low-quality state and resists recovery.
The institutional design literature does not ask how its proposals must change when drafting cost approaches zero and the false-positive rate rises continuously with adoption.
We close these gaps by connecting all four channels---$c(a)$ falling, $V$ rising, $K_{rev}$ lagging, and $\lambda(a)$ rising---to the long-run state of the publishing system, characterising conditions under which quality collapses suddenly and is hard to restore, and deriving which institutional levers can prevent or reverse that shift.

\section{Baseline Model}
\label{sec:model}

\paragraph{Players and strategies.}
We consider a population of $N$ authors submitting to a single journal over repeated periods.
Each manuscript has binary quality: good ($q=1$) with probability $\alpha$, and bad ($q=0$) with probability $1-\alpha$.
Under replicator dynamics, intermediate submission strategies are evolutionarily unstable and are eliminated in the mean-field limit, reducing the effective strategy space to two options \citep{Weibull1995, Hofbauer1998}.
Under \textbf{selective submission} (OG), an author submits only when $q=1$.
Under \textbf{universal submission} (AS), an author submits regardless of quality, hoping that imperfect screening allows bad papers through.
We write $x \in [0,1]$ for the share of authors playing OG; the remaining $1-x$ play AS.

\paragraph{Two bottlenecks.}
The publication process has two distinct chokepoints; each creates a different strategic force.
The first is \textbf{review capacity} $K_{rev}$: the number of papers that can receive careful scrutiny per period.
Submission volume is $V(x) = N[1-(1-\alpha)x]$, which rises as the AS share grows.
When $V$ exceeds $K_{rev}$, the available reviewer time must be spread across more submissions: each paper receives less careful attention, and the effective depth of scrutiny per manuscript falls \citep{Bianchi2018, Kovanis2016}.
The \textbf{screening intensity} is:
\begin{equation}
    \eta(x) = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{K_{rev}}{V(x)}\right).
    \label{eq:eta}
\end{equation}
The second bottleneck is \textbf{publication capacity} $K$: the number of slots the journal can fill per period.
Papers that pass review form a peer-accepted pool $M(x)$; at most $K$ are published.
The probability that a peer-accepted paper is ultimately published---the \textbf{rationing probability}---is:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\rho}(x) = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{K}{M(x)}\right).
    \label{eq:rho}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Screening errors and the spam payoff.}
Peer review is an imperfect filter \citep{Bornmann2010, Cicchetti1991}.
A good paper is falsely rejected with probability $\epsilon$; a bad paper wrongly passes with probability $\lambda$.
Both error rates apply at full scrutiny.
When $\eta < 1$, reviewer time per paper is diluted: each submission receives only a fraction $\eta$ of the careful attention required to apply the full filter, so the effective pass rates rise above their full-scrutiny values \citep{Bianchi2018}:
\begin{equation}
    \pi_0(x) = 1 - \eta(x)(1-\lambda), \qquad \pi_1(x) = 1 - \eta(x)\,\epsilon.
    \label{eq:pass_rates}
\end{equation}
As $\eta \to 0$, both rates converge to 1: the journal loses all discriminatory power because reviewers cannot give any paper the attention the filter requires.
The peer-accepted pool is $M(x) = N\alpha\,\pi_1(x) + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\,\pi_0(x)$.
Because OG and AS authors submit good papers on identical terms, the strategic difference reduces to the net value of submitting a bad paper:
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_{bad}(x) = r \cdot \bar{\rho}(x) \cdot \pi_0(x) - c,
    \label{eq:pibad}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the publication reward and $c$ the submission cost.
We work in the region $\lambda r < c < r(1-\epsilon)$: without congestion, submitting a bad paper is unprofitable, but the incentive reactivates once screening degrades---the same adverse-selection logic as in Akerlof's market for lemons \citep{Akerlof1970}.

\paragraph{Dynamics.}
The expected per-period fitness of each strategy differs only in the treatment of bad papers.
An OG author submits only when $q=1$ (probability $\alpha$), earning expected fitness $f_{OG} = \alpha[r\bar{\rho}\,\pi_1 - c]$.
An AS author submits regardless, earning $f_{AS} = \alpha[r\bar{\rho}\,\pi_1 - c] + (1-\alpha)[r\bar{\rho}\,\pi_0 - c]$.
The fitness advantage of AS over OG is therefore $f_{AS} - f_{OG} = (1-\alpha)\,\Pi_{bad}(x)$.
Substituting into the standard two-strategy replicator equation $\dot{x} = x(1-x)[f_{OG}-f_{AS}]$ gives:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{x} = -x(1-x)(1-\alpha)\,\Pi_{bad}(x).
    \label{eq:replicator}
\end{equation}
When $\Pi_{bad} > 0$, AS spreads and $x$ falls; when $\Pi_{bad} < 0$, OG spreads and $x$ rises.
Two forces compete as the AS share grows.
First, rising volume degrades screening ($\eta$ falls, $\pi_0$ rises): submitting bad papers becomes more attractive.
Second, rising volume floods the publication queue ($\bar{\rho}$ falls): the publication lottery becomes harder to win.
When the screening-collapse force dominates, the system tips toward full corruption.
When the rationing force dominates, extreme slot scarcity deters spam even after screening has failed---a mechanism we call \emph{defence by congestion}.

\paragraph{Four long-run outcomes.}
The signs of $\Pi_{bad}$ at the two boundary states determine which long-run outcome the system reaches.
Table~\ref{tab:regime_classification} summarises the four cases.
A key structural observation is that the two boundaries are controlled by different parameters: the honest boundary ($x=1$) is secured by keeping $\lambda$ low, while the corrupt boundary ($x=0$) is held by keeping $K$ tight.
This means the two bottlenecks can be targeted separately as policy instruments---a point we return to in Section~\ref{sec:analysis}.
Figure~\ref{fig:replicator_dynamics} illustrates the bistable case.
$\Pi_{bad}$ is positive near $x=0$ (corruption reinforces itself) and negative near $x=1$ (honesty reinforces itself), with an unstable tipping threshold at $x^* \approx 0.80$ separating the two regions.
Which long-run outcome prevails depends entirely on where the system starts relative to this threshold.

\section{The AI Extension}
\label{sec:ai_extension}

\paragraph{AI adoption as a parameter shift.}
Generative AI affects the publication system through multiple channels simultaneously.
We capture this with a single scalar $a \in [0,1]$, where $a = 0$ denotes the pre-AI baseline and $a = 1$ denotes full adoption across the author population.
The parameter can be interpreted as the field-level intensity of AI use---a composite of the fraction of authors employing AI tools and the depth of their integration into the writing and submission workflow.
We treat $a$ as a comparative-statics parameter: we hold $a$ fixed and trace how the equilibrium structure from Section~\ref{sec:model} changes as $a$ deepens.
As established in Section~\ref{sec:stylized_facts} and illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism}, AI acts through four distinct channels, and the channels are not symmetric.

\paragraph{Channel 1---Production: more submissions at every composition.}
The most direct effect of AI is to lower the marginal cost of drafting and preparing a manuscript, which raises total submission volume at any given composition of the author population.
Randomised experiments find that LLM access cuts professional writing time by roughly 40\% \citep{NoyZhang2023, StokelWalker2023}.
We formalise this as a multiplicative shift in total submission volume:
\begin{equation}
    V(x, a) \;=\; N\bigl[1-(1-\alpha)x\bigr]\cdot(1 + \gamma a),
    \label{eq:V_a}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma > 0$ is the \textbf{production elasticity}.
System-level data confirm this: monthly arXiv submissions rose roughly 50\% in two years, with over one-fifth of preprints showing measurable LLM modification \citep{arXiv2024, Liang2025}.
$V$ rises with $a$ regardless of $x$: AS authors, whose constraint was drafting effort rather than ideas, now prepare and submit low-quality manuscripts they would not previously have bothered producing; OG authors face a lower marginal cost of bringing a manuscript to submission-ready quality, converting preliminary work into formal submissions faster.
The underlying quality probability $\alpha$ of any individual manuscript remains fixed; what changes is the number of submission-ready manuscripts each author produces per period.

\paragraph{Channel 2---Friction: lower cost of submitting bad papers.}
Beyond raising volume, AI directly lowers the cost of each submission attempt.
We write:
\begin{equation}
    c(a) \;=\; c_0(1 - \phi\, a), \qquad \phi \in (0,1),
    \label{eq:c_a}
\end{equation}
where $c_0$ is the baseline cost and $\phi$ governs the rate at which AI erodes submission friction.
The cost reduction enters $\Pi_{bad}$ directly: a lower $c$ raises the net payoff from submitting a bad paper even if acceptance probabilities are unchanged.
The effect is also asymmetric across strategies, though the asymmetry is not per-paper: $c$ enters both $\Pi_{bad}$ and $\Pi_{good}$ symmetrically as a per-submission cost.
The asymmetry arises from submission frequency.
OG authors submit only when they have genuine work ready, so the total cost reduction they realise each period is small.
AS authors submit everything---good papers and bad---so the same per-paper reduction compounds across a much larger submission stream, directly amplifying the flood incentive.

\paragraph{Channel 3---Vulnerability: weaker screening signal.}
AI-generated text is harder to distinguish on surface features \citep{StokelWalker2023}, AI-assisted reviews award systematically higher scores \citep{Latona2024, Liang2024ICML}, and automated detection remains unreliable---false-negative rates exceed 70\% \citep{Liang2023detectors}.
Each mechanism raises the probability that a bad paper passes review.
We write:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda(a) \;=\; \lambda_0 + \mu\, a, \qquad \mu \geq 0,
    \label{eq:lambda_a}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_0$ is the baseline false-positive rate and $\mu \geq 0$ is the sensitivity of screening quality to AI adoption.
$\lambda(a)$ is a reduced-form parameter: it captures the net effect of AI adoption on the false-positive rate without modelling each mechanism separately.
Rising $\lambda$ enters $\pi_0 = 1 - \eta(1-\lambda)$ directly: even a carefully reviewed paper is more likely to pass.

\paragraph{Channel 4---Review assistance: capacity grows, but more slowly.}
AI can also assist reviewers---summarising papers, flagging inconsistencies, checking statistical reporting---so review capacity is not fixed.
We write:
\begin{equation}
    K_{rev}(a) \;=\; K_{rev}(0)\cdot(1 + \delta\, a),
    \label{eq:Krev_a}
\end{equation}
where $\delta \geq 0$ is the \textbf{verification elasticity}.
However, deep verification remains human-intensive: confidentiality rules restrict AI use in reviews \citep{Naddaf2025, Checco2021}, and reviewer capacity was already strained before the AI era \citep{Meyerson2025}.
These constraints enforce the asymmetry $\gamma > \delta \geq 0$ (Equation~\ref{eq:asymmetry}).

\paragraph{The combined effect: spam becomes more profitable.}
Substituting the four channels into the baseline model yields the AI-extended spam payoff:
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_{bad}(x, a) \;=\; r\cdot\bar{\rho}(x,a)\cdot\pi_0(x,a) \;-\; c(a),
    \label{eq:pibad_a}
\end{equation}
where $\eta(x,a) = \min\!\left(1, K_{rev}(a)/V(x,a)\right)$, $\;\pi_0(x,a) = 1 - \eta(x,a)(1-\lambda(a))$, $\bar{\rho}(x,a) = \min\!\left(1, K/M(x,a)\right)$, and
\begin{equation}
  M(x,a) \;=\; (1+\gamma a)\bigl[N\alpha\,\pi_1(x,a) + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\,\pi_0(x,a)\bigr].
  \label{eq:M_ai}
\end{equation}
The factor $(1+\gamma a)$ reflects that AI inflates the total pool of papers reaching the publication queue proportionally to the same volume multiplier that governs $V(x,a)$: higher submission rates translate into a larger peer-accepted pool for any given pass rates $\pi_0$, $\pi_1$.
This introduces a countervailing stabilising force.
A larger $M$ tightens slot rationing ($\bar{\rho} = K/M$ falls), reducing each spammer's probability of publication.
Formally, $V\uparrow\;\Rightarrow\;M\uparrow\;\Rightarrow\;\bar{\rho}\downarrow\;\Rightarrow\;\Pi_{bad}\downarrow$: the very flood of submissions that degrades screening also clogs the publication queue.
Note that this congestion effect compresses the \emph{revenue} term $r\bar{\rho}\pi_0$ rather than raising the submission cost $c(a)$, which falls with $a$.
Whether the destabilising channels ($\pi_0\uparrow$, $c\downarrow$) or the stabilising channel ($\bar{\rho}\downarrow$) dominates depends on the relative magnitudes of $K$ and $M$: when $K\gg M$ rationing is slack and the destabilising forces prevail; when $K\approx M$ the congestion defence is active.
To isolate the mechanism most cleanly, consider the honest boundary $x = 1$, where only good papers are submitted and slot rationing is typically absent ($\bar{\rho}(1,a) \approx 1$ when publication slots exceed honest-author output):
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_{bad}(1, a) \;\approx\; r\cdot\pi_0(1,a) \;-\; c(a).
    \label{eq:pibad_boundary}
\end{equation}
At $x = 1$, only good papers are submitted, so submission volume equals $V(1,a) = N\alpha(1+\gamma a)$.
For both calibration sets this remains strictly below $K_{rev}(a)$ for all $a \in [0,1]$, so $\eta(1,a) = 1$: review capacity is not the binding constraint at the honest boundary.\footnote{For Set~A: $K_{rev}(a) = 300(1+0.1a) > 200(1+0.5a) = V(1,a)$ requires $a < 10/7 \approx 1.43$, well above the maximum adoption level.}
The bad-paper pass rate therefore reduces to $\pi_0(1,a) = \lambda(a)$, and the GH condition at $x=1$ simplifies to $\lambda(a) < c(a)/r$.
The operative mechanism at the honest boundary is not review congestion but the erosion of the screening signal relative to the cost of submission: $\lambda(a)$ rises while $c(a)/r$ falls simultaneously.
Provided $\mu \geq 0$ and $\phi > 0$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \Pi_{bad}(1,a)}{\partial a} \;=\; r\underbrace{\mu}_{>0\;\text{(rising }\lambda\text{)}} \;\underbrace{+\,c_0\phi}_{>0\;\text{(falling }c\text{)}} \;>\; 0.
    \label{eq:pibad_slope}
\end{equation}
Setting $\Pi_{bad}(1,a^*) = 0$ gives a closed-form tipping point $a^* = (c_0 - r\lambda_0)/(r\mu + c_0\phi)$; for Set~A, $a^* = (25 - 10)/(20 + 10) = 0.50$.
Above $a^*$, $\Pi_{bad}(1,a) > 0$: the honest long-run outcome at $x=1$ is no longer stable, and the system transitions from bistability into Global Corruption (or from GH into bistability if the corrupt boundary was previously deterred).

\paragraph{Creation versus verification: why AI is structurally asymmetric.}
The four-channel decomposition shows that AI exerts pressure from both the supply and quality dimensions simultaneously.
Channels 1 and 2 affect the supply side: they make it cheaper and faster to produce manuscripts.
Channel 3 affects the screening side: it makes it harder to distinguish good submissions from bad ones at the review stage.
Channel 4 is the only force working against the other three, but it is structurally constrained to grow more slowly.
Writing is largely a syntactic task that language models perform well; evaluation is a semantic and domain-specific task requiring human expertise, data access, and contextual judgment \citep{Checco2021}.
Institutions designed for the pre-AI era assumed rough parity between production and verification rates.
That assumption no longer holds, and the asymmetry is not a temporary calibration issue---it reflects a fundamental difference in what AI can automate.\footnote{We hold $\alpha$ (the probability a manuscript is genuinely good) fixed throughout the formal analysis.
In principle, AI may also raise $\alpha$ via a quality-uplift channel: writing $\alpha(a) = \alpha_0(1+\xi a)$ would add a partially offsetting force.
Higher $\alpha$ increases the share of genuine work in the submission pool, which raises $M(x,a)$ and tightens slot rationing $\bar{\rho}$, thereby reducing $\Pi_{bad}$.
However, the GH stability condition at $x=1$ reduces to $\lambda(a) < c(a)/r$ when $\eta=1$---a condition that does not involve $\alpha$---so quality uplift does not shift the closed-form tipping threshold $a^*$.
Evidence that AI raises genuine research quality is mixed and modest in magnitude \citep{NoyZhang2023}; we therefore treat $\alpha$ as fixed to focus on the four identified destabilising channels.}

\paragraph{Special cases: when stability can be preserved.}
Not every AI adoption trajectory leads to quality collapse.
Equation~\eqref{eq:pibad_boundary} shows that the Global Honesty condition can persist even at high $a$ if two circumstances hold jointly.
First, if $\delta \approx \gamma$---review capacity scales nearly as fast as submission volume---then $\eta(1,a)$ does not fall and the congestion channel is neutralised.
This could arise if structured AI-assisted verification pipelines (automated code checks, data-consistency audits) allow reviewers to handle more submissions without sacrificing depth.
Second, if $\mu \approx 0$---AI tools do not degrade screening reliability, perhaps because structured reporting requirements close the gap that polished AI writing opens---then $\lambda(a)$ does not rise.
If both conditions hold simultaneously, the cost reduction alone is typically insufficient to cross the tipping threshold, because $c(a)$ falls gradually while the honesty condition $c(a) > r\lambda(a)$ can remain satisfied.
Table~\ref{tab:scenario_matrix} maps the four combinations of these two conditions onto qualitative predictions about regime direction.
The critical implication is that the institutional response determines which cell the system occupies: $a$ is largely exogenous, but $\delta$ and $\mu$ are policy choices.

\paragraph{Testable predictions.}
The AI extension generates four empirically distinguishable predictions.
First, quality degradation should be \emph{nonlinear} in AI adoption: the system holds near the honest long-run outcome while $a$ remains below the tipping threshold, then shifts abruptly rather than gradually.
Smooth, continuous quality decline would be inconsistent with the bistable structure.
Second, fields with higher AI adoption should show review-congestion signatures---longer turnaround times, rising desk-rejection rates, declining reviewer acceptance rates---as proxies for falling $\eta$ \citep{Meyerson2025}.
Third, acceptance rates should rise in fields with widespread AI-assisted reviewing, independently of any improvement in underlying research quality, as a signature of rising $\lambda$ \citep{Latona2024}.
Fourth, expanding publication slots should have \emph{asymmetric} effects: before tipping, more slots increase access; after tipping, more slots accelerate quality collapse by raising the spam payoff $\Pi_{bad}$.
We examine the regime consequences of each lever in Section~\ref{sec:analysis}.

\section{Equilibria, Tipping, and Hysteresis under AI Adoption}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Equilibrium Conditions and the AI Shift}

The long-run outcome of the publishing system is determined by the signs of the spam payoff $\Pi_{bad}$ at two boundary states.
At the \emph{honest boundary} ($x = 1$), almost all authors are selective; the question is whether a single deviating author can profitably flood the system.
At the \emph{corrupt boundary} ($x = 0$), almost all authors submit everything; the question is whether the system can self-correct.
These two boundaries are governed by different parameters.
As established in Section~\ref{sec:model}, the honest boundary is secured by keeping $\lambda$ low; the corrupt boundary is held by keeping $K$ tight enough that the publication lottery deters spam even under degraded screening.

We define publication quality as the share of genuinely good papers among those accepted:
\begin{equation}
    Q(x) \;=\; \frac{N\alpha\,\pi_1(x)}{M(x)} \;=\; \frac{\alpha\,\pi_1(x)}{\alpha\,\pi_1(x) + (1-\alpha)(1-x)\,\pi_0(x)}.
    \label{eq:quality}
\end{equation}
Since slot rationing applies uniformly to the peer-accepted pool $M(x)$, it does not alter the ratio of good to bad papers; quality is therefore determined entirely by the composition $x$ at the long-run outcome.
The four possible long-run outcomes---GH, GC, BS, SC---and their quality levels are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:regime_classification}.

Under AI adoption $a > 0$, four simultaneous shifts push $\Pi_{bad}(x, a)$ upward at every interior $x$.
Rising submission volume (elasticity $\gamma$) and lagging review capacity (elasticity $\delta < \gamma$) lower the screening intensity $\eta(x, a)$, raising the bad-paper pass rate $\pi_0(x, a)$.
Falling submission cost $c(a)$ directly raises the net payoff from each submission attempt.
Rising false-positive rate $\lambda(a)$ further degrades the review filter even for papers that receive full scrutiny.
The combined derivative (Equation~\ref{eq:pibad_slope}) is strictly positive under the asymmetry $\gamma > \delta$: AI makes spamming more attractive at every composition of the author population.
The analysis in this section traces the structural consequences of this shift for regime boundaries, stability windows, and recovery conditions.

We use two representative parameter sets throughout (Table~\ref{tab:calibration}).
Set~A ($K_{rev}=300$, $K=400$, $r=100$, $c=25$, $\lambda=0.1$) represents a typical journal prone to bistability, where the review bottleneck is the binding constraint.
Set~B ($K_{rev}=1200$, $K=180$, $r=150$, $c=30$, $\lambda=0.4$) represents a top-tier journal where extreme slot scarcity is the defining constraint.
AI elasticity parameters are fixed at $\gamma=0.5$, $\delta=0.1$, $\phi=0.4$, $\mu=0.2$ throughout.

\subsection{How AI Shifts Regime Boundaries}

Figure~\ref{fig:ai_phase_diagram} presents the core comparative-statics result.
The left panel shows the baseline ($a = 0$) regime map across the $(K_{rev}, K)$ parameter space.
The right panel shows the same map under high AI adoption ($a = 0.8$).
The comparison is unambiguous: the Global Honesty (green) region shrinks substantially, the Bistability (yellow) and Global Corruption (red) zones expand and shift left, and the Stable Coexistence (blue) region contracts.
A journal safely in the GH zone at baseline may find itself in BS or GC at $a = 0.8$ without any change in its own institutional parameters.

The magnitude of the shift is quantifiable.
At $a = 0.8$ with $\gamma = 0.5$ and $\delta = 0.1$, submission volume has risen by 40\% while review capacity has risen by only 8\%.
A journal with $K_{rev} = 300$ has the effective screening power of a journal with $K_{rev} \approx 231$ at baseline---a loss of nearly one-quarter of screening capacity with no change in institutional design.
To restore the same screening intensity, the journal would need to raise $K_{rev}$ to approximately $389$, a 30\% increase in review resources.

\paragraph{The slot expansion paradox is amplified by AI.}
Expanding publication slots ($K$) seems like an obvious response to submission pressure.
Our model shows this response backfires when verification does not scale proportionally.
Increasing $K$ raises the rationing probability $\bar{\rho}(x, a)$, which raises the expected payoff from submitting a bad paper.
This effect is amplified under AI adoption: when $c(a)$ is already low and $\lambda(a)$ is already elevated, even a modest improvement in the publication lottery is enough to push $\Pi_{bad}(1, a)$ from negative to positive, tipping the system from GH into Bistability.
More slots increase the prize in a game whose entry barrier has already been eroded by AI.
This is visible in Figure~\ref{fig:ai_phase_diagram}: moving upward (increasing $K$) in the right panel is more dangerous than the same move in the left panel.
The slot expansion paradox identified in the baseline model is not merely preserved under AI adoption---it is exacerbated.

\paragraph{Defence by congestion and its limits.}
Extreme slot scarcity can stabilize an otherwise fragile system.
When $K$ is small relative to the peer-accepted pool $M$, the rationing probability $\bar{\rho}$ is so low that the expected payoff from submitting a bad paper falls below the submission cost even under degraded screening---the journal's publication lottery is simply too hard to win.
This defence operates through the condition $\bar{\rho}(0, a) \cdot \pi_0(0, a) < c(a)/r$, and all three terms move against it as $a$ rises.
Rising $\pi_0(0, a)$ from degraded screening and rising $\lambda$ increases the left side.
Falling $c(a)$ reduces the right side.
The net effect is that a journal relying on slot scarcity needs progressively tighter $K$ to maintain the same deterrent effect as $a$ increases.
At some point, $K$ must be so low that the participation constraint for honest authors---$\Pi_{good}(1, a) > 0$---is violated, pushing the system into the No Submission zone.
Defence by congestion is a real stabilising force, but it is not robust to sustained increases in $a$.

\subsection{The False-Positive Rate as Critical Destabiliser}

Among the four AI channels, rising $\lambda(a)$ is structurally the most dangerous.
The GH condition at $x = 1$ requires $\Pi_{bad}(1, a) < 0$; when screening is fully effective ($\eta = 1$), this simplifies to $\lambda < c(a)/r$.
Under baseline parameters ($c = 25$, $r = 100$), this requires $\lambda < 0.25$.
A slope of $\mu = 0.2$ over full adoption ($a = 1$) pushes $\lambda$ from $0.10$ to $0.30$, crossing this threshold and making GH structurally impossible regardless of review capacity.
No investment in $K_{rev}$ can restore GH once $\lambda \geq c/r$, because even a perfectly reviewed bad paper passes at rate $\lambda$ and yields a positive expected payoff.
This result is consistent with signalling theory: the reliability of the screening signal---not merely the volume of screening---is what sustains the separating equilibrium \citep{Tiokhin2021, Ellison2002}.

The false-positive channel also explains why AI-assisted reviewing is particularly concerning.
AI-assisted reviews have been shown to award systematically higher scores, raising acceptance rates \citep{Latona2024, Liang2024ICML}.
If widespread AI use in reviewing raises the average $\lambda$, the effect is direct and structural: the GH boundary shifts inward regardless of what authors do.
Interventions that target $\lambda$ directly---structured reporting checklists, mandatory data and code deposition, statistical audit requirements---therefore address the most critical vulnerability in the system.

\subsection{Policy Levers and Summary Propositions}

Each parameter corresponds to a concrete institutional intervention.
We summarise the mapping in four propositions; formal derivations are in the Appendix.

\begin{description}

\item[Proposition 1 (Monotone destabilisation).]
\textit{Under the asymmetry $\gamma > \delta \geq 0$:
(i)~\textbf{Honest boundary.}
$\Pi_{bad}(1,a)$ is strictly increasing in $a$ (analytically, for all parameter values satisfying the model assumptions).
The Global Honesty region in $(K_{rev},K)$ space shrinks strictly as $a$ rises, and there exists a finite closed-form threshold
$a^{*} = (c_0 - r\lambda_0)/(r\mu + c_0\phi) \in (0,\infty)$
above which no $(K_{rev},K)$ combination sustains GH without additional institutional intervention.
(ii)~\textbf{Interior compositions.}
For $x\in(0,1)$, $\partial\Pi_{bad}/\partial a>0$ holds analytically when review is uncongested ($\eta=1$, Case~I) or rationing is inactive ($\bar\rho=1$, Case~II).
When both constraints bind (Case~III), the result holds under a sufficient condition on the screening signal (stated in the Appendix) and is confirmed numerically throughout $(x,a)\in(0,1)\times[0,1]$ for both calibrations in Table~\ref{tab:calibration}.
All three cases jointly imply that the GH region shrinks as $a$ rises.}

\item[Proposition 2 (Slot expansion paradox).]
\textit{Holding $K_{rev}$ and $a$ fixed, increasing $K$ weakly raises $\Pi_{bad}(x, a)$ at every $x$.
The marginal destabilising effect of an additional slot is strictly larger at higher $a$, because lower $c(a)$ and higher $\lambda(a)$ amplify the gain from winning the publication lottery.
The paradox is therefore amplified, not attenuated, by AI adoption.}

\item[Proposition 3 (Erosion of congestion defence).]
\textit{The corrupt-boundary stability condition $\Pi_{bad}(0, a) < 0$ holds if and only if $K < K^{*}(a) \equiv c(a)M(0,a)/[r\pi_0(0,a)]$.
When the cost-reduction slope satisfies $\phi \geq \gamma$, $K^{*}(a)$ is strictly decreasing in $a$ for all $a \in [0,1]$ analytically.
When $\gamma > \phi$ (as in the calibrations of Table~\ref{tab:calibration}), $K^{*}(a)$ is strictly decreasing for $a \geq (\gamma-\phi)/(2\phi\gamma)$ analytically and is confirmed numerically to be strictly decreasing throughout $[0,1]$ under both Sets~A and~B.
Consequently, a journal that relied on passive slot scarcity for protection at baseline may need to actively reduce $K$ to maintain the same deterrent as $a$ rises.}

\item[Proposition 4 (Policy lever ranking).]
\textit{Among the primary levers, reducing $\lambda$ (verification quality) and raising $K_{rev}$ (review capacity) are unconditionally beneficial: they expand the GH region without side effects.
Raising $c$ (submission friction) restores the honesty condition but may deter some honest authors.
Reducing $K$ (tightening slots) provides passive deterrence but risks the No Submission zone.
Expanding $K$ alone is the only intervention that can make the system strictly worse.}

\end{description}

\paragraph{Early warning signals.}
The model identifies observable signatures that precede a tipping-point collapse.
Rising submission-to-review ratios ($V / K_{rev}$) signal declining screening intensity $\eta$.
Lengthening turnaround times and increasing desk-rejection rates indicate reviewer overload and the onset of rubber-stamping.
Acceptance rates rising faster than improvements in underlying research quality signal a rising effective $\lambda$.
In fields where AI adoption is high, these signals should appear before any observable quality decline, because the system can remain near $x \approx 1$ while the tipping threshold $x^*_{tip}$ rises toward the current honest-author share.
To make this concrete: under Set~A parameters, $x^* \approx 0.80$ at baseline, leaving an apparent safety margin of only 0.20 from the fully honest state.
As $a$ rises, $x^*$ climbs monotonically toward $x = 1$, so this margin closes continuously---from 0.20 toward zero---while published quality appears unchanged, because the population remains above the threshold throughout.
By the time $a$ reaches $a^* \approx 0.50$, the margin has vanished and the system tips into Global Corruption with no visible warning in quality metrics.
The gap between observable quality (still high) and structural fragility (rapidly growing) is precisely what makes AI-induced tipping difficult to detect in advance.
We examine the testability of these predictions in Section~\ref{sec:predictions}.


\section{Agent-Based Modeling}
\label{sec:abm}

\subsection{Why Simulate? Heterogeneity and the Diffusion of AI Adoption}

The analytical results in Section~\ref{sec:analysis} derive from a deterministic, infinite-population mean-field approximation \citep{Hofbauer1998}.
Two features of real academic communities are suppressed by this approximation.
First, AI adoption is not uniform: early adopters, late adopters, and non-adopters coexist within any field, and the overall adoption level $a$ rises gradually through a diffusion process analogous to the spread of other general-purpose technologies \citep{Rogers2003, Bass1969}.
Second, finite populations are subject to stochastic fluctuations that can push a system across a tipping threshold even when the deterministic drift would not \citep{Nowak2006, Traulsen2009}.
An agent-based model (ABM) allows us to test whether our three main results---gradual onset of fragility, discrete tipping, and severe path dependence---survive both of these complications \citep{Bonabeau2002, Epstein1996}.

\subsection{Model Design}

We simulate a population of $N = 1000$ authors.
Each author plays one of two strategies: selective submission (OG) or universal submission (AS); intermediate submission strategies are excluded by the evolutionary instability result of Section~\ref{sec:model}.
The journal's review capacity and quality parameters evolve with the field-level AI adoption level $a_t$, which rises exogenously along a linear ramp from $a = 0$ to $a = 0.8$ over the simulation horizon---a stylised representation of the diffusion trajectory documented empirically \citep{arXiv2024, Liang2025}.
At each step, the effective parameters $K_{rev}(a_t)$, $\lambda(a_t)$, and $c(a_t)$ are updated according to the functional forms in Section~\ref{sec:ai_extension}.
Strategy revision follows the pairwise Fermi comparison process \citep{Szabo1998, Traulsen2009}: a randomly chosen agent $i$ compares its payoff $\Pi_i$ with that of a randomly drawn peer $j$ and switches with probability
\begin{equation}
    P(s_i \to s_j) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\!\bigl(-\beta\,[\Pi_j - \Pi_i]\bigr)},
\end{equation}
where $\beta = 1.0$ governs selection intensity.
This rule captures bounded rationality: better-performing strategies spread, but noisily.

\subsection{Results: Tipping as AI Adoption Rises}

Figure~\ref{fig:tipping_timeseries} reports the core dynamic result.
Panel~(a) traces the tipping threshold $x^*(a_t)$---the unstable boundary in the bistability zone---as $a_t$ rises.
The threshold climbs monotonically from $x^* \approx 0.80$ at baseline toward $x = 1$ as $a$ approaches the analytical limit $a^* \approx 0.50$ derived above.
The system appears healthy throughout: observed honest-author shares remain at $x \approx 1$.
Yet a fixed-size shock of $\Delta x = 0.18$---representing a coordinated wave of low-quality submissions, of the kind documented at major publishers during paper-mill incidents \citep{Bik2016}---has opposite consequences depending on timing.
Applied early at $a \approx 0.08$, it drops $x$ to $0.82$, which is still above $x^*(0.08) \approx 0.80$; the honesty norm self-repairs within a few hundred steps.
Applied late at $a \approx 0.53$---just past the critical threshold $a^* \approx 0.50$ at which $\Pi_{bad}(1,a)$ turns positive and $x=1$ loses stability---the system has entered Global Corruption.
The identical shock now causes permanent collapse not because $x$ is pushed below an interior threshold, but because no stable honest long-run outcome exists at all: any downward perturbation from $x=1$ leads irreversibly to $x=0$.
The \emph{safety margin}---the gap between the current honest-author share $x_t$ and the rising threshold $x^*(a_t)$---therefore shrinks silently while observable quality remains high, and vanishes entirely as $a$ crosses $a^*$.
This is precisely the ``quiet before the cliff'' signature that catastrophic-shift theory predicts for systems approaching a bifurcation \citep{Scheffer2009}.

Panel~(b) confirms path dependence at $a = 0.45$ (bistable regime).
Two trajectories separated by only $\Delta x_0 = 0.08$ diverge to opposite absorbing states: the run starting just above $x^*(0.45) \approx 0.92$ converges to full honesty within a few hundred steps, while the run starting just below collapses immediately.
No endogenous force can reverse this collapse; the population is trapped in the low-quality long-run outcome \citep{Schelling1978, Scheffer2001}.

\subsection{Robustness: Varying $\delta/\gamma$ and $\mu$}

Both panels are evaluated at $a = 0.8$, representing sustained high AI adoption.

Panel~(a) shows the long-run honest-author share $x^*$ on a continuous colour scale.
The parameter space is dominated by the collapse region ($x^* \approx 0$, dark red); a stable-honesty zone ($x^* \approx 1$, dark green) exists only in the bottom-right corner, where $\delta/\gamma \gtrsim 0.5$ and $\mu \lesssim 0.08$ simultaneously.
Panel~(b) confirms this pattern in terms of regime classification.
The baseline calibration ($\delta/\gamma = 0.2$, $\mu = 0.2$, marked by white dotted lines) lies solidly inside the Global Corruption zone.
The Bistability region (yellow) is confined to a narrow band at $\mu \lesssim 0.09$, independent of $\delta/\gamma$.
The Stable Coexistence and Global Honesty regions are accessible only when both conditions hold jointly; satisfying one alone is insufficient.

Two findings from this robustness check are particularly important.
First, the $\mu$ dimension is the binding constraint: even if $\delta/\gamma = 1$ (review capacity scales perfectly with submission volume), the system remains in Global Corruption whenever $\mu > 0.09$.
No investment in review staffing can compensate for a screening process that cannot distinguish polished AI text from genuine contributions \citep{Checco2021, Liang2023detectors}.
Second, the boundary between BS and GC is nearly vertical in the right panel, meaning that raising $\delta/\gamma$ from 0.2 to 0.5 shifts the system from GC to BS (where recovery is at least possible) only if $\mu$ is already low.
This interaction confirms that the two policy levers---raising $K_{rev}$ and reducing $\lambda$---must be deployed together, not sequentially.

\subsection{Stochastic ABM: Does Finite-Population Noise Validate the ODE?}

The ODE assumes an infinitely large, well-mixed population and therefore suppresses all randomness.
Real academic communities are finite.
Even a system that sits nominally above the tipping threshold can cross it by chance when the number of authors is small \citep{Traulsen2009, Nowak2006}.
This subsection tests whether the deterministic tipping results of Section~\ref{sec:analysis} survive under finite-population noise, and whether community size changes that verdict.

We extend the batch-matching ABM from \citet{Bonabeau2002} to incorporate the same rising AI adoption schedule used in Section~\ref{sec:abm} ($a_t$ linear from 0 to 0.8 over 3{,}000 steps).
At each step $t$, the local screening capacity is $K_{rev,\text{eff}}(a_t) = K_{rev,0}\cdot(1+\delta a_t)/(1+\gamma a_t)$, the false-positive rate is $\lambda(a_t)$, the submission cost is $c(a_t)$, and the paper pool reaching the publication queue scales as $M(x,a_t)=(1+\gamma a_t)\cdot M_{\text{base}}(x,a_t)$---identical functional forms to the ODE\@.
The only departure from the ODE is that $N$ is finite and strategy revision is stochastic: each revision attempt uses the Fermi rule \citep{Szabo1998}, so the population does not move deterministically toward its expected next state but takes a noisy step.
To isolate pure demographic noise---the $1/\!\sqrt{N}$ fluctuations inherent to any finite population---we set batch size equal to $N$ in all conditions, so every agent participates in each round and no additional batch-level heterogeneity is introduced.
We run 100 independent replications for each of three community sizes---$N = 1{,}000$ (a large generalist field), $N = 500$ (a mid-size specialised field), and $N = 200$ (a small sub-field)---under two starting conditions that bracket the practical range of initial safety margins.
The \emph{margin scenario} starts at $x_0 = 0.82$, which is 0.02 above the initial tipping threshold $x^*(a=0) = 0.80$: a thin but nominally positive buffer.
The \emph{no-margin scenario} starts at $x_0 = x^*(0) = 0.80$, exactly at the tipping threshold, where the ODE's deterministic drift is zero and any noise can immediately push the system toward collapse.

Figure~\ref{fig:abm_validation} presents results across three panels.
Panel~(a) shows the margin scenario ($x_0 = 0.82$).
The ODE rises steadily toward $x \approx 1$: starting 0.02 above the tipping threshold, deterministic drift points toward full honesty.
All three ABM means track the ODE closely, converging to $x \approx 1$ within roughly 200 steps regardless of community size.
The 0.02 safety buffer is therefore universally protective: even the smallest community ($N = 200$) is not pushed below the threshold by demographic noise of order $1/\!\sqrt{N}$, because the buffer exceeds the typical fluctuation amplitude at all three population sizes tested.

Panel~(b) removes the buffer entirely ($x_0 = x^*(0) = 0.80$).
The ODE now declines toward $x \approx 0$: with no margin, deterministic drift immediately points downward as $a$ rises above zero.
The ABM tells a qualitatively different story.
All three community sizes decline sharply in the first 200--300 steps but then stabilise at an intermediate coexistence level---$N = 1{,}000$ near $x \approx 0.40$, $N = 500$ near $x \approx 0.47$, $N = 200$ near $x \approx 0.45$---well above the ODE's near-zero equilibrium.
This divergence reveals a structural limitation of the mean-field approximation: in a finite population, stochastic Fermi updating sustains a mixed-strategy steady state that the ODE cannot recover.
Community size has only a minor and non-monotone effect on the terminal level, confirming that population size is not the primary determinant of outcomes once the margin has been lost.

Panel~(c) quantifies both scenarios via collapse-fraction curves (collapse defined as $x < 0.3$, 100 replications).
In the margin scenario (solid lines), the collapse fraction is zero for all three community sizes across all adoption levels: no run crosses the collapse threshold, confirming that the 0.02 buffer is a robust shield regardless of $N$.
In the no-margin scenario (faded lines), the collapse fraction jumps to approximately $55$--$60\%$ at very low adoption levels ($a \approx 0.05$) and then plateaus.
The three $N$ values are tightly clustered throughout; $N = 1{,}000$ sits marginally above the smaller communities, consistent with larger populations converging more faithfully toward the ODE's lower equilibrium.

Three conclusions follow.
First, the safety margin $\Delta = x_0 - x^*(0)$ operates as an effective binary shield: a margin of even 0.02 prevents any collapse across all community sizes tested, while the complete absence of a margin exposes roughly half of all runs to quality decline irrespective of $N$.
Second, the ODE overestimates collapse severity when the margin is lost: finite-population stochasticity stabilises the system at a partial coexistence state ($x \approx 0.40$--$0.48$) rather than the ODE's near-zero long-run outcome, because Fermi noise sustains a residual share of honest authors that deterministic dynamics cannot maintain.
Third, community size is not a meaningful safeguard in either scenario: it neither rescues small fields when a margin exists (all recover regardless) nor provides meaningful protection when it does not (all decline to a similar partial-coexistence level).
Institutional design must therefore focus on actively maintaining a positive safety margin; community size alone cannot substitute for it.

\section{Institutional Responses}
\label{sec:institutions}

The three results from Section~\ref{sec:analysis} reduce the institutional problem to a single condition: keep $\Pi_{bad}(x, a)$ negative.
AI adoption pushes $\Pi_{bad}$ upward through four channels simultaneously: submission volume rises, review capacity lags, submission cost falls, and the false-positive rate $\lambda$ increases.
Effective policy must counteract each channel.
We organize the response around five principles, ordered by the tightness of the constraint each relieves.

\textbf{Principle 1: Scale verification ($K_{rev}\uparrow$).}
Higher review capacity raises the screening intensity $\eta = \min(1, K_{rev}/V)$, which reduces the bad-paper pass rate $\pi_0$ and lowers $\Pi_{bad}$ at the corrupt boundary $x \approx 0$.
Practical mechanisms include structured reviewer panels with explicit recognition credits \citep{Aczel2021}, AI-assisted consistency and format pre-screening that frees expert time for substantive judgment \citep{Checco2021}, and co-review programmes that train junior reviewers alongside senior ones.
However, there is a binding caveat: when $\lambda > c(a)/r$, even perfect screening ($\eta = 1$) cannot make $\Pi_{bad}(1) < 0$ at the honest boundary.
At $a = 0.8$ under baseline parameters, $\lambda = 0.26$ while $c(a)/r = 0.17$; no feasible increase in $K_{rev}$ alone can stabilize the system at $K = 400$.
Crucially, review assistance tools must not themselves raise $\lambda$: format and consistency checks are appropriate, but automated quality scoring is not \citep{Naddaf2025}.

\textbf{Principle 2: Reduce the false-positive rate ($\lambda\downarrow$) — the binding constraint.}
The GH condition at the honest boundary requires $\Pi_{bad}(1) < 0$, which holds if and only if $\lambda < c(a)/r$.
This is the binding constraint: no amount of $K_{rev}$ expansion or slot restriction restores GH while $\lambda$ exceeds this threshold.
Three mechanisms directly reduce $\lambda$.
First, mandatory data, code, and materials sharing makes fabrication costlier and enables post-publication verification \citep{Munafo2017, Nosek2015}.
Second, structured reporting checklists---CONSORT for randomised trials \citep{Moher2009}, PRISMA for systematic reviews---force authors to document methods in ways that make gaps visible to reviewers.
Third, Registered Reports commit authors to their hypotheses and analysis plan before results are known, eliminating outcome-selective reporting \citep{Nosek2018}, which is itself a leading source of inflated false-positive rates \citep{Simmons2011}.
Taken together, these mechanisms address the root cause that $\lambda$ is elevated: reviewers cannot easily distinguish genuine contributions from polished but uninformative manuscripts.

\textbf{Principle 3: Add smart friction ($c\uparrow$).}
The GH condition also requires $c > r \cdot \lambda$.
As AI adoption lowers $c(a) = c_0(1 - \phi a)$, this gap closes and eventually inverts.
Submission tokens or annual per-journal quotas raise the effective cost of marginal submissions without requiring any detection of AI use \citep{Cotton2013}.
Escalating resubmission fees impose additional costs on the low-quality cycling of rejected papers through multiple journals.
We note an equity caveat: blunt friction raises costs for all authors, including those at under-resourced institutions; targeted exemptions are discussed in the final paragraph of this section.

\textbf{Principle 4: Decouple dissemination from certification.}
The current publishing architecture conflates two distinct functions: disseminating findings quickly and certifying quality carefully.
When certification degrades under submission pressure, both functions fail together.
Separating the two---via preprint servers combined with overlay journals or community platforms such as Peer Community In \citep{Ginsparg2011}---allows dissemination to remain immediate while certification proceeds at the pace required for genuine scrutiny.
The slot-expansion paradox established in Section~\ref{sec:analysis} applies specifically to certified publication slots $K_{cert}$, not to preprint availability.
Decoupling therefore prevents the paradox from emerging: adding preprint capacity does not raise $\Pi_{bad}$, because preprint posting is not the payoff-generating event in the spam incentive.

\textbf{Principle 5: Reshape evaluation rewards ($r$ reduced for marginal output).}
The spam payoff scales linearly with $r$: $\Pi_{bad} = r \cdot \bar{\rho} \cdot \pi_0 - c$.
Current career and grant structures treat paper counts as a primary productivity metric \citep{Smaldino2016, casadevall2014reforming}, effectively maximising the effective reward for any accepted paper regardless of quality.
Impact-weighted or reproducibility-indexed assessment would reduce the effective $r$ for marginal submissions while preserving incentives for high-quality work \citep{Stephan2012}.
The GH condition at $x=1$ requires $\lambda(a) < c(a)/r$; reducing $r$ directly widens this margin, enlarging the parameter region in which the honest long-run outcome is self-sustaining.
Reward reform operates on a longer horizon than Principles~1--4 and requires coordination across funders, institutions, and evaluation bodies; it is therefore a deep-redesign lever rather than a quick win.

Table~\ref{tab:institutions} maps each mechanism to its primary parameter shift, and Figure~\ref{fig:interventions} illustrates the joint effect of the principles in the $(K_{rev}, K)$ parameter space.
The left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:interventions} shows that at $a = 0.8$ under baseline parameters, Global Corruption dominates most of the space, and Set~A sits deep in the GC region; the navy arrow shows that raising $K_{rev}$ alone (Principle~1) leaves Set~A in GC because $\lambda = 0.26 > c/r = 0.17$.

The lesson is that Principles~2 and~3 are necessary to exit GC, while Principle~1 converts bistability into global honesty: the three levers are complements, not substitutes.

\textbf{Transition policy: resetting after tipping.}
Hysteresis means that gradual, journal-by-journal reforms are insufficient once the system has tipped into Global Corruption.
In the GC long-run outcome, $\Pi_{bad} > 0$ for all values of the honest-author share $x$; no individual author has an incentive to deviate back toward honesty \citep{Scheffer2001}.
Recovery requires a large, coordinated intervention that simultaneously restores $\lambda$, raises $c$, and expands $K_{rev}$ enough to shift the entire parameter configuration out of the GC region.
The ClinicalTrials.gov registration mandate, which required prospective trial registration as a condition of journal publication, illustrates the scale of coordination needed \citep{Moher2009}: a single policy change, applied across many journals simultaneously, moved an entire field.
The implication for AI-era publishing is that funders and professional societies must act across journal families, not journal by journal.

\textbf{Equity.}
Friction mechanisms ($c\uparrow$) and strict verification requirements ($\lambda\downarrow$) impose larger burdens on authors at under-resourced institutions, who may lack the data-sharing infrastructure, statistical support, or time to meet demanding new standards.
Smart design can preserve the incentive effects while redistributing the cost: submission token grants for authors at lower-income institutions \citep{Cotton2013}; structured reporting checklists, which carry low infrastructure cost, preferred over mandatory open data repositories, which carry high infrastructure cost; subsidized reviewer development programmes that build review capacity where it is currently thin \citep{Aczel2021}.
These equity adjustments do not change the core model predictions---the regime boundaries shift on the same dimensions---but they change who bears the adjustment cost.

\textbf{Policy menu: quick wins and deep redesign.}
Some mechanisms require no inter-journal coordination and can be adopted immediately: structured reporting checklists, mandatory data and code sharing policies, submission token caps, and escalating resubmission fees.
Others require collective action across journals or fields: overlay certification architectures, cross-journal quota systems, and coordinated field-wide mandates.
The right sequencing strategy is to deploy the quick wins first, to arrest the upward drift of $\Pi_{bad}$, while building the coordination capacity needed for deep redesign before any tipping event occurs.
Waiting for a quality crisis before acting is the worst strategy: recovering from GC requires a larger simultaneous push than preventing the tip in the first place \citep{Scheffer2009}.

\section{Welfare Analysis and Normative Policy Design}
\label{sec:welfare}

\paragraph{Social welfare in a certification system.}
The publishing system produces two social goods beyond private author payoffs.
First, it generates \emph{scientific knowledge} as a public good: verified results can be used by downstream researchers, educators, practitioners, and policymakers at zero marginal cost, without depleting the original discovery \citep{DasguptaDavid1994}.
Second, it provides a \emph{certification signal}: a credential that allows readers to distinguish reliable findings from unreliable ones without re-examining every manuscript.
Degradation of this signal imposes social costs that no individual author internalises.
We define a reduced-form social welfare function:
\begin{equation}
  W(x,a) \;=\; \underbrace{N\bigl[x\,\Pi_{good}(x,a) + (1-x)\,\Pi_{bad}(x,a)\bigr]}_{\text{aggregate author surplus}}
  \;+\; \underbrace{S \cdot Q(x,a) \cdot \bar{M}(x,a)}_{\text{social value of certified knowledge}}
  \;-\; \underbrace{C(K_{rev})}_{\text{verification cost}},
  \label{eq:welfare}
\end{equation}
where $S > 0$ is the per-paper social value when quality is perfect, $Q(x,a) \equiv \alpha\pi_1 / [\alpha\pi_1 + (1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0/(x + (1-x))]$ is the precision of the certified pool (the fraction of accepted papers that are genuinely good), $\bar{M}(x,a)$ is total papers accepted per period, and $C(K_{rev})$ is the institutional cost of maintaining review capacity.
Welfare increases with $Q$ because a certified pool dominated by genuine contributions generates reliable knowledge cumulation, while $Q \approx 0$ produces noise that misdirects subsequent research and policy \citep{Ioannidis2005}.

\paragraph{The two externalities: why private and social incentives diverge.}
The individual spam payoff $\Pi_{bad}(x,a) = r\bar{\rho}\pi_0 - c$ omits two costs imposed on others.
The first is a \emph{congestion externality}.
When a spam author submits, total submission volume $V$ rises, which depresses screening intensity $\eta = \min(1, K_{rev}/V)$ and lowers the review quality received by every other submitter.
Peer-review attention is a finite shared resource, and individual overuse degrades the commons \citep{Ostrom1990}: each spam submission captures private value while imposing a diffuse cost on all other authors and editors.
The second is a \emph{certification externality}.
A low-quality paper, once accepted, reduces the precision $Q$ of the certified pool and degrades the informativeness of the certification signal for all downstream readers.
This is the quality-dilution mechanism in \citet{Akerlof1970}'s market for lemons: sellers of bad goods contaminate the quality signal, raising doubts about good goods and eroding the value of the entire market.
In academic publishing, the ``market'' is the pool of certified papers, the ``buyers'' are readers and downstream researchers, and ``bad goods'' are low-quality manuscripts that passed degraded peer review.
Neither externality is priced by the individual submitter.
The divergence between private and social returns is the canonical justification for corrective policy \citep{Pigou1920}.
Spamming is privately rational when $\Pi_{bad}(x,a) > 0$, but the full social cost includes the congestion externality imposed on honest authors and the certification-quality loss imposed on readers.
The socially optimal submission norm is therefore $x = 1$ (global honesty): this maximises $Q$, minimises wasteful review effort, and generates the highest social value from the certification pool.
Yet $x = 1$ is privately self-sustaining only when $\Pi_{bad}(1,a) < 0$; whenever AI adoption raises $\Pi_{bad}$ above zero, the social optimum and the private Nash equilibrium diverge.

\paragraph{Welfare ranking of the four regimes.}
Table~\ref{tab:welfare_regimes} ranks the four stable regimes by social welfare.
In GH ($x^* = 1$), no bad papers are submitted, the certified pool consists entirely of genuine work ($Q = 1$), and review resources are used efficiently.
In GC ($x^* = 0$), all authors submit bad papers alongside good ones, the certified pool is dominated by spam papers ($Q$ falls sharply under degraded screening), and the certification signal carries minimal information.
The welfare gap $\Delta W = W(1,a) - W(0,a) > 0$ is strictly positive even accounting for the higher author surpluses in GC, because the social-value term $S \cdot Q \cdot \bar{M}$ collapses in GC while the congestion cost of maintaining a large review apparatus to process a flood of mostly bad papers remains.
In SC ($x^* \in (0,1)$), welfare lies strictly between the two extremes.
In BS, expected welfare is a probability-weighted average of $W(1,a)$ and $W(0,a)$ determined by initial conditions and stochastic shocks: bistability is not itself a welfare outcome but a risk of tipping into GC from a currently-healthy GH state.

\paragraph{Hysteresis amplifies the welfare loss.}
The welfare cost of AI adoption is not simply the static difference $W(1,a) - W(0,a)$; it is amplified by the hysteresis dynamic.
Once the system tips from GH into GC, restoring GH requires a large simultaneous intervention across $\lambda$, $K_{rev}$, and $c$.
This transition is costly: review infrastructure must be rebuilt, submission friction must be re-imposed, and reporting norms must be rehabilitated, all while the system is already in a low-quality long-run outcome that discourages investment in verification.
The ex-ante social cost of failing to prevent tipping therefore includes both the static welfare gap $\Delta W$ and the transition cost of recovery---a cost that is entirely avoided if preventive institutions are maintained \citep{Scheffer2001}.
This welfare-dynamic argument provides the theoretical foundation for the urgency of early intervention: the expected social benefit of preventing a tipping event is strictly larger than the expected benefit of correcting one after it occurs, because the correction requires additional resources and inflicts additional welfare losses during the recovery transition.

\paragraph{Normative policy recommendations.}
The welfare analysis converts the five institutional principles of Section~\ref{sec:institutions} into a normative hierarchy ordered by welfare efficiency.
Four Corollaries follow from combining the welfare function~\eqref{eq:welfare} with Propositions 1--4.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Corollary W1 (Corrective levy on spam externalities).}
Because spam submissions impose unpriced negative externalities---congestion costs on honest authors and certification-quality losses on readers---the welfare-maximising policy imposes a corrective (Pigouvian) levy per submission equal to the marginal social cost of a spam attempt \citep{Pigou1920}.
In the model, this levy is equivalent to raising $c$: it restores the condition $c > r\lambda(a)$ that keeps GH stable, without restricting honest submissions if the levy is calibrated to the spam pass rate $\pi_0$ rather than applied uniformly.
Submission tokens and per-journal annual quotas are practical approximations of this levy: the normative case for them is not administrative convenience but welfare-theoretic---they correct a market failure, not merely a nuisance \citep{Cotton2013}.
An equity-preserving design issues token grants to authors at under-resourced institutions, ensuring that the levy targets strategic spamming rather than participation barriers.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Corollary W2 (Peer review as a public good requiring public funding).}
Peer review generates a public good: the certification signal is non-excludable (once published, the quality verdict is available to all readers) and the benefit of a thorough review is non-rival (every reader gains without diminishing others' access) \citep{DasguptaDavid1994}.
Private provision of public goods is sub-optimal under standard conditions: individual reviewers and journals do not capture the full social return from careful review, and competitive pressure to cut costs erodes voluntary supply below the socially efficient level.
The welfare-efficient remedy is public investment in $K_{rev}$---targeted grants for reviewer development programmes, structured-review recognition schemes, and journal subsidies for methods review---rather than relying on uncompensated voluntarism.
\citet{Aczel2021} propose transparent reviewer recognition systems as a coordination device that raises effective $K_{rev}$ without proportional budget increases; the normative case for mandating such systems is the public-good argument, not merely editorial preference.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Corollary W3 (Coordination mandate in the bistable region).}
Proposition~4 establishes that reducing $\lambda$ and raising $K_{rev}$ are unconditionally welfare-improving: they lower spam payoffs without harming honest authors.
However, decentralised adoption of verification standards is a coordination game with multiple equilibria: if competing journals do not adopt structured reporting requirements, rejected spam papers cycle to lower-tier venues, and no single journal captures the system-level benefit of its own investment in quality.
This cross-journal externality means the welfare-maximising outcome---universal verification standards---is not a Nash equilibrium of decentralised journal competition \citep{Ostrom1990}.
A mandatory field-wide standard, enforced by funders, accreditation bodies, or professional associations, internalises the cross-journal spillover and restores the welfare-efficient outcome.
The ClinicalTrials.gov registration mandate, which required prospective trial registration as a publication condition across many journals simultaneously, demonstrates that such coordination is institutionally feasible and welfare-improving even when no single journal would adopt the standard unilaterally \citep{Moher2009}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Corollary W4 (Welfare ranking of institutional levers).}
The four levers of Proposition~4 have distinct welfare profiles.
First, reducing $\lambda$ (structured reporting, mandatory data sharing) is a \emph{Pareto improvement} relative to the unregulated equilibrium: it raises $Q^*$ and raises honest-author surplus by easing competition from spam, while imposing no direct cost on honest authors who already meet the new standard.
Second, raising $K_{rev}$ (public investment in review capacity) is welfare-improving when the congestion constraint is binding; it improves $Q^*$ and reduces the review burden per paper, with a cost borne by funders rather than authors.
Third, raising $c$ (Pigouvian levy) improves welfare when the spam externality cost exceeds the honest-author surplus loss from higher submission costs, which holds whenever $\Pi_{bad}(x,a) > 0$ and the honest-author share is substantial; the equity caveat is that the levy must be accompanied by targeted exemptions to avoid deterring genuine science.
Fourth, raising $K$ (expanding publication slots) is the \emph{unique lever that unconditionally reduces welfare}: it increases $\Pi_{bad}$ (Proposition~2), lowers $Q^*$ by attracting more spam, and degrades the certification signal for all readers, with no offsetting benefit.
The normative implication is unambiguous: expanding publication slot capacity without simultaneously reducing $\lambda$ is a welfare-reducing response to submission pressure, even though it appears to relieve editorial backlogs in the short run.
Backlogs are a symptom of submission congestion; expanding slots without fixing the underlying screening deficit treats the symptom while worsening the disease.

\begin{tcolorbox}[boxstyle, title={Box 4\quad Normative Policy Agenda: Welfare Priorities and Justifications}]
\small
\noindent\textbf{Welfare objective:}\;
Maximise $W(x,a) = \text{author surplus} + S \cdot Q \cdot \bar{M} - C(K_{rev})$ subject to the system converging to Global Honesty (GH), not Global Corruption (GC).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Market failures requiring correction:}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep, topsep=2pt, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textit{Congestion externality}: each spam submission degrades review quality for all other authors by tightening $\eta = K_{rev}/V$.
  \item \textit{Certification externality}: each accepted spam paper dilutes the certified pool precision $Q$, reducing the value of certification for all readers (Akerlof lemons mechanism).
  \item \textit{Public-good underprovision}: peer review is non-rival and non-excludable; private voluntary supply is systematically below the socially optimal $K_{rev}$.
  \item \textit{Coordination failure}: no single journal internalises cross-journal quality spillovers; decentralised adoption of verification standards is sub-optimal.
\end{itemize}

The GH long-run outcome is both privately stable (when $\Pi_{bad} < 0$) and socially optimal.

Welfare-efficient policy closes this gap by raising the private cost of spamming and the private return to honesty, not by expanding slots or ignoring the screening deficit.

\section{Empirical Predictions and Measurement}
\label{sec:predictions}

\paragraph{Prediction 1: Quality degradation is nonlinear---quiet, then a cliff.}
The bistable structure of the model generates a specific observable signature.
Quality metrics should hold approximately steady while AI adoption $a$ rises below the tipping threshold, then deteriorate rapidly once the tipping threshold $x^*(a)$ reaches the current honest-author share.
Smooth, continuous quality decline would be inconsistent with the model: the bistable structure produces a cliff, not a slope.
The early-warning indicators are not quality itself but the shrinking gap between the current author composition $x_t$ and the rising tipping threshold $x^*(a_t)$---a gap that narrows silently while quality appears stable \citep{Scheffer2009}.
Empirical evidence is consistent with this reading.
Monthly arXiv submissions rose approximately 50\% between late 2022 and late 2024, while reviewer acceptance rates and post-publication retraction counts have not yet stabilised at new long-run levels \citep{arXiv2024, VanNoorden2023}.
Field-level heterogeneity provides additional structure: \citet{Kobak2025} find AI-modified text rates exceeding 40\% in some biomedical sub-disciplines while remaining near zero in others, suggesting that different fields sit at different distances from their respective tipping thresholds---some already past it, others still in the quiet phase.

\paragraph{Prediction 2: The gap between collapse and recovery thresholds widens as AI adoption rises.}
As $a$ increases, Proposition~3 shows that the defence-by-congestion mechanism requires progressively tighter slot constraints to hold the corrupt boundary, while Proposition~1 shows the Global Honesty boundary erodes.
The model therefore predicts that the intervention required to restore quality in a field that has tipped is strictly larger than the intervention that would have prevented the tip.
Pre-AI evidence establishes that the hysteresis mechanism itself is empirically real, independently of what triggers the initial collapse.
\citet{Bik2016} document that image duplication---driven by paper mills, not generative AI---became entrenched in specific biomedical sub-fields despite sustained editorial intervention; \citet{VanNoorden2023} report that annual retraction counts exceeded 10{,}000 for the first time in 2023 without receding, suggesting that the low-quality long-run outcome sustains itself once established.
The pre-AI paper-mill literature thus confirms the mechanism: quality collapses are not self-correcting, and removing the proximate trigger (the mills) does not restore the parameter configuration that originally supported honest submission.
The AI-specific prediction is that the same mechanism will be triggered at lower adoption thresholds and across more fields simultaneously, because AI lowers $c(a)$ and raises $\lambda(a)$ for the entire author population rather than for a subset of bad actors.
A direct test would compare the institutional effort required to restore quality indicators in a field that tipped---whether by paper mills before 2022 or by AI-driven flooding after---against one that never tipped but faces equivalent submission pressure.

\paragraph{Prediction 3: Proxies for a rising false-positive rate.}
A rising $\lambda$ produces a detectable mismatch between surface quality and substantive reliability: manuscripts become more polished while methodological soundness does not improve proportionately.
Three observable proxies track this channel.
First, \textbf{acceptance rates} should rise faster than any improvement in underlying research quality, because easier-to-pass manuscripts inflate the acceptance pool without proportionate quality gains; \citet{Latona2024} document this experimentally---AI-assisted reviews award systematically higher scores.
Second, \textbf{surface linguistic quality} should improve independently of replication success or citation impact.
\citet{Kobak2025} show that AI modification is measurable through excess vocabulary, providing a continuous field-level proxy for surface polish that can be compared against downstream quality indicators.
Third, \textbf{replication rates} should decline in fields with high AI adoption if the screening signal is genuinely degrading.
The Open Science Collaboration found that fewer than half of 100 psychology studies replicated successfully \citep{OSC2015}; replicating that audit with papers published after 2022 in high-AI-adoption fields, and comparing against low-adoption fields, would directly test whether the false-positive channel is active.

\paragraph{Prediction 4: Proxies for review-capacity stress.}
The model predicts declining screening intensity $\eta = \min(1, K_{rev}/V)$ as submission volume grows faster than review capacity.
Four observable proxies track this channel.
First, \textbf{reviewer invitation acceptance rates} should decline faster in high-AI-adoption fields; \citet{Meyerson2025} document a continuous 21-year decline at a single journal, and the model predicts this trajectory steepens as AI-driven submission pressure intensifies.
Second, \textbf{editorial turnaround times} should lengthen as editors wait longer for reviewer responses, signalling scarcity in the review pool.
Third, \textbf{desk rejection rates} should rise as editorial offices pre-filter to protect reviewers from overload; an increasing share of submissions receiving no substantive evaluation is a direct proxy for $\eta$ falling toward zero for that fraction of papers.
Fourth, \textbf{reviewer concentration} should increase---a growing Gini coefficient on review counts across the active reviewer pool indicates that a shrinking fraction of researchers is absorbing a rising fraction of the total burden, a pattern that preceded the current shortage \citep{Kovanis2016}.

\paragraph{Prediction 5: Data sources and identification strategy.}
Testing these predictions requires field-level panel data on both the production and verification sides of the system.
Several existing sources can be combined.
On the production side, arXiv submission records are public, monthly, and tagged by subfield; the LLM modification classifier of \citet{Liang2025}, applied to these records, provides a continuous field-level estimate of $a_t$.
The vocabulary-based detection approach of \citet{Kobak2025}, applied across the 15-million-abstract PubMed archive, offers an independent measure of AI modification at the sub-discipline level.
On the verification side, reviewer acceptance rate series can be constructed from editorial logs at journals that publish annual statistics; acceptance rates, turnaround times, and retraction counts are available at scale through OpenAlex, CrossRef, and the Retraction Watch database.
The natural experiment for causal identification is the release of ChatGPT in November 2022, which created a sharp and field-heterogeneous shock to submission cost $c$.
A difference-in-differences design would compare fields with high ex-ante AI amenability---measured by pre-2022 adoption of computational tools, large collaborative networks, or text-heavy output---against low-amenability fields, before and after November 2022.
The prediction is that high-amenability fields show faster growth in submission-to-review ratios, rising acceptance rates, and more rapid accumulation of post-publication retractions relative to low-amenability fields, controlling for field size and pre-shock submission trends.

\paragraph{Prediction 6: What would falsify the asymmetry hypothesis.}
The model's conclusions rest on two empirical claims: production scales faster than verification ($\gamma > \delta$), and the false-positive rate rises with AI adoption ($\mu > 0$).
Either claim failing empirically would revise the institutional implications.
If $\delta \approx \gamma$---if AI-assisted reviewing tools raise review capacity at nearly the same rate as AI raises submission volume---the congestion channel would not activate.
This would be observable as stable submission-to-acceptance ratios and constant turnaround times despite rising submission volumes in high-adoption fields.
If $\mu \approx 0$---if AI tools improve screening reliability, perhaps because structured AI-assisted checklists close the gap that polished writing opens---the false-positive channel would be neutralised.
This would be observable as stable or falling acceptance rates in high-AI-adoption fields combined with stable replication rates for newly published work.
The robustness analysis in Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap_outcomes} shows that $\mu$ is the binding parameter: even if $\delta/\gamma$ rises toward 1, the system remains in Global Corruption whenever $\mu > 0.09$.
A finding that AI-assisted reviewing consistently \emph{improves} score calibration---rather than inflating it---would therefore be the strongest possible disconfirmation of the model's policy conclusions \citep{Checco2021}.
The available evidence currently points in the opposite direction \citep{Latona2024, Liang2024ICML, Naddaf2025}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Scope and simplifying assumptions.}
The model makes two structural choices that bound its applicability.
First, we reduce the strategy space to two options: submit only good work, or submit regardless of quality.
Real authors calibrate effort continuously, and intermediate strategies can coexist in finite communities.
The binary reduction is justified by an evolutionary instability argument \citep{Weibull1995}: at any population state $x$, a strategy that adds bad-paper submissions earns strictly higher fitness when $\Pi_{bad} > 0$, and strictly lower fitness when $\Pi_{bad} < 0$, so intermediate submission rates are unstable under replicator dynamics and the population is driven to one of the two extremes in the mean-field limit.
In practice, a continuum of effort levels would smooth the tipping threshold into a region rather than a sharp boundary, making early warning signals harder to detect but not eliminating them.
Second, we model a single journal with fixed capacity.
In reality, rejected papers cascade down a prestige hierarchy: \citet{Siler2015} find that roughly 62\,\% of papers rejected at three high-impact journals were eventually published elsewhere.
This cascade distributes submission pressure across tiers rather than absorbing it.
A multi-journal extension would predict that tipping first occurs at lower-tier venues---which typically have smaller $K_{rev}$ and weaker screening---and then propagates upward as the pool of credibly certified papers shrinks.
Our single-journal results are therefore conservative: the threshold at which quality degrades across the system as a whole is likely lower than the threshold we identify for any single venue.

\paragraph{AI adoption as a scalar: limits and heterogeneity.}
We represent AI adoption with a single scalar $a$ that aggregates the fraction of authors using AI tools and the intensity of their use.
This captures the mean effect but suppresses two empirically important dimensions.
The first is task heterogeneity.
AI excels at drafting and reformatting text but falls short of replicating the judgment required for novel empirical analysis \citep{Checco2021}.
The production elasticity $\gamma$ therefore measures AI's effect on submission volume rather than on genuine scientific output.
A model that separates writing assistance from research assistance would generate sharper predictions about $\mu$: fields where AI mainly improves surface fluency face a higher $\mu$ than fields where AI genuinely improves research quality.
The second dimension is cross-author heterogeneity in adoption timing.
Early adopters gain a first-mover advantage under current publication incentives, and social learning then accelerates diffusion through the same imitation dynamics documented for other general-purpose technologies \citep{Rogers2003}.
As Section~\ref{sec:abm} shows, stochastic fluctuations from a concentrated wave of intensive early adopters can push a finite community below its tipping threshold before field-wide mean adoption reaches a critical level.
Heterogeneous adoption therefore makes tipping events more likely and less predictable than the scalar analysis implies.

\paragraph{The problem is institutional mismatch, not AI adoption itself.}
The model's prescriptions do not depend on AI being harmful to science in aggregate.
If review capacity scaled at the same rate as submission volume---$\delta = \gamma$---and AI tools improved screening reliability rather than degrading it---$\mu \leq 0$---the Global Honesty region would expand under rising $a$, not contract.
\citet{NoyZhang2023} document genuine productivity gains from AI assistance; the issue is not that AI exists but that the gap $\gamma - \delta > 0$ exists.
\citet{AcemogluRestrepo2019} provide a useful framing: automation raises productivity in tasks that are routine and codifiable while increasing demand for tasks requiring contextual judgment and domain expertise.
Text generation is largely the former; verifying the validity of a novel empirical claim is largely the latter.
The challenge for publishing institutions is therefore not to restrict AI adoption---which would be both unenforceable and wasteful where AI genuinely helps---but to close the gap between $\gamma$ and $\delta$ through targeted investment in verification infrastructure.
The scenario matrix in Table~\ref{tab:scenario_matrix} makes this concrete: which cell a field occupies is an institutional choice, not a technological fate.
Fields that invest in structured AI-assisted verification pipelines, mandatory data deposition, and reviewer development programmes can remain in the upper-left cell even as $a$ rises.
Fields that do not will drift toward the upper-right.

\paragraph{Multi-journal competition and quality cascades.}
Extending the model to a hierarchy of competing journals would capture dynamics absent from our analysis.
When authors face multiple venues, rejection does not dissipate submission pressure---it routes it to the next tier.
\citet{Clauset2015} document steep and persistent prestige hierarchies in academic hiring networks; analogous hierarchies characterise journals, where a small number of high-impact venues confer disproportionate career rewards $r$.
In a tiered model, the slot expansion paradox would have a cross-venue counterpart: entry of new low-tier journals raises total certification capacity $K$ without improving screening quality $\lambda$, raising $\Pi_{bad}$ at every tier simultaneously.
\citet{Zollman2024} show that prestige-maximising incentives in multi-journal settings already reward strategic over honest behaviour; AI adoption amplifies this dynamic by lowering $c(a)$ and raising $\lambda(a)$ for the entire author population.
The institutional implication is that verification standards must be coordinated across journal tiers.
A single journal raising $K_{rev}$ and lowering $\lambda$ provides limited system-level protection if rejected spam papers are absorbed by lower-tier venues and ultimately circulate as certified work.
Overlay certification models---where a shared preprint layer is evaluated by tier-specific panels with explicit quality standards---are one architecture that could enforce coordination without requiring every journal to act simultaneously.

\paragraph{Endogenous rewards, reviewer effort, and reputation systems.}
Two parameters we treat as fixed---the publication reward $r$ and the reviewer participation rate embedded in $K_{rev}$---are endogenous to institutional design, and relaxing this assumption would enrich the model.
On the reward side, $r$ reflects career-level incentives to publish.
Evaluation frameworks that weight publication counts heavily inflate $r$ for marginal submissions; shifting toward impact-based or reproducibility-weighted assessment directly reduces $r$ and shrinks the parameter region where $\Pi_{bad} > 0$ \citep{Hicks2015}.
\citet{Stephan2012} traces publication inflation to career structures that treat paper counts as a proxy for scientific value; AI adoption compounds this pre-existing incentive problem by making low-quality output cheaper to produce.
On the reviewer side, we model $K_{rev}$ as a hard capacity constraint, but reviewer effort per paper is also a margin.
Reviewer participation has declined continuously for two decades \citep{Meyerson2025}, and the decline accelerates if quality collapse erodes the perceived value of certification---a positive feedback loop that our model does not capture.
An extension treating reviewer effort as endogenous would predict that transparency mechanisms---publishing review reports, providing structured evaluation templates, recognising reviewer contributions \citep{Aczel2021}---can raise effective screening accuracy without increasing the number of reviewers.
The two levers---more reviewers and better effort per reviewer---address the same capacity constraint $K_{rev}$ through different margins and should be designed jointly.
Neither is sufficient in isolation when $\lambda > c(a)/r$, the binding condition identified in Section~\ref{sec:analysis}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have studied what happens to a publishing system when AI raises the productivity of manuscript production faster than it raises the capacity to verify manuscripts.
The central asymmetry---production elasticity $\gamma$ exceeding verification elasticity $\delta$---is not a modelling assumption but an empirical claim supported by documented submission growth, documented reviewer decline, and experimental evidence on AI writing productivity \citep{NoyZhang2023, arXiv2024, Meyerson2025}.
Three results follow from this asymmetry.
First, extreme slot scarcity can stabilise the honesty norm under degraded screening, but AI erodes this protection continuously by lowering the submission cost $c(a)$: the required slot tightness grows with adoption and eventually forces the system into the No Submission zone.
Second, gradual AI adoption can trigger a discrete collapse rather than a smooth decline; the system can appear healthy---with observable quality near its maximum---while the gap between the current author composition and the tipping threshold silently narrows.
Third, once the system tips, recovery is harder than prevention, because the low-quality long-run outcome is self-sustaining: no individual author has a reason to deviate back toward honesty, and small interventions cannot overcome the strategic lock-in \citep{Scheffer2009}.
Expanding publication slots accelerates rather than relieves this dynamic, because it raises the expected payoff of submitting low-quality work without addressing the underlying screening deficit.

Translating these results into institutional design requires targeting the parameters that matter most.
The false-positive rate $\lambda$ is the binding constraint: when $\lambda > c(a)/r$, no feasible increase in review staffing can restore the Global Honesty condition, because even carefully reviewed low-quality manuscripts pass at a rate that makes submission individually rational.
Effective policy must therefore reduce $\lambda$ directly---through mandatory data and code sharing, structured reporting requirements, and registered study designs---while simultaneously raising $K_{rev}$ and restoring submission friction \citep{Munafo2017, Cotton2013}.
These three levers are complements, not substitutes: the analysis in Section~\ref{sec:institutions} shows that $\lambda$ reduction is necessary to exit the Global Corruption region, and $K_{rev}$ expansion is necessary to convert bistability into Global Honesty.
The institutional response to AI is therefore not "more slots" but a different kind of institution---one in which verification is a first-class resource, not an afterthought.

The window for preventive action is narrower than it appears.
Because the system can remain near the honest long-run outcome while structural fragility accumulates, the observable quality signal gives no reliable warning before a tipping event \citep{Scheffer2001}.
Fields that invest in verification infrastructure, reviewer incentives, and submission friction before AI adoption deepens can preserve the integrity of certification at moderate cost.
Fields that wait for a visible quality crisis will face a recovery problem that requires a larger and more disruptive coordinated intervention---the equivalent of a field-wide mandate rather than a journal-level policy.
The asymmetry between production and verification is not temporary: it reflects a fundamental difference in what language models can automate, and institutions designed for an earlier era will need deliberate redesign to remain effective.

\section{Proofs of Propositions~1--4}
\label{app:proofs}

The four propositions in Section~\ref{sec:analysis} characterise how the spam payoff $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(x,a)$ responds to the AI adoption parameter~$a$ and to the four institutional levers $\lambda$, $K_{\mathit{rev}}$, $c$, and $K$.
This appendix provides complete formal derivations.
We first collect notation and establish three lemmas that are used repeatedly across the proofs.

\paragraph{Notation.}

Throughout, $x \in [0,1]$ is the share of selective (OG) authors and $a \in [0,1]$ is the AI adoption intensity.
Define:
\begin{align*}
  V_0(x)              &\;\equiv\; N\bigl[1-(1-\alpha)x\bigr]
    && \text{baseline submission volume (at $a=0$),}\\
  V(x,a)              &\;=\; V_0(x)(1+\gamma a)
    && \text{total submission volume,}\\
  K_{\mathit{rev}}(a) &\;=\; K_{\mathit{rev}}(0)(1+\delta a)
    && \text{review capacity,}\\
  \eta(x,a)           &\;=\; \min\!\Bigl(1,\;\tfrac{K_{\mathit{rev}}(a)}{V(x,a)}\Bigr)
    && \text{screening intensity,}\\
  \pi_0(x,a)          &\;=\; 1 - \eta(x,a)\bigl(1-\lambda(a)\bigr)
    && \text{bad-paper pass rate,}\\
  \pi_1(x,a)          &\;=\; 1 - \eta(x,a)\,\varepsilon
    && \text{good-paper pass rate,}\\
  M(x,a)              &\;=\; (1+\gamma a)\bigl[N\alpha\,\pi_1(x,a) + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\,\pi_0(x,a)\bigr]
    && \text{peer-accepted pool,}\\
  \bar\rho(x,a)       &\;=\; \min\!\Bigl(1,\;\tfrac{K}{M(x,a)}\Bigr)
    && \text{rationing probability,}\\
  \Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(x,a) &\;=\; r\,\bar\rho(x,a)\,\pi_0(x,a) - c(a)
    && \text{spam payoff.}
\end{align*}

We work throughout under the \textbf{AI asymmetry assumption} $\gamma > \delta \geq 0$ and the parametric assumptions $\mu \geq 0$, $\phi \in (0,1)$, $r, c_0 > 0$.
We also impose the \textbf{discriminatory filter condition}:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:app_filter}
  \lambda(a) + \varepsilon < 1 \qquad \text{for all } a \in [0,1].
\end{equation}
This states that peer review rejects bad papers at a strictly higher rate than it falsely rejects good ones, i.e.\ the filter is better than random.
The proofs split into three cases based on which capacity constraints are binding:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Case~I} (uncongested): $V(x,a) \leq K_{\mathit{rev}}(a)$, so $\eta = 1$.
  \item \textbf{Case~II} (congested, unrationed): $V > K_{\mathit{rev}}$ and $M \leq K$, so $\eta < 1$ and $\bar\rho = 1$.
  \item \textbf{Case~III} (congested and rationed): $V > K_{\mathit{rev}}$ and $M > K$, so $\eta < 1$ and $\bar\rho = K/M < 1$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Lemma A.1 (Screening intensity falls with $a$).}

\textit{In Cases~II and~III:}
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:app_deta}
  \frac{\partial\eta}{\partial a}
  = \frac{K_{\mathit{rev}}(0)\,(\delta-\gamma)}{V_0(x)\,(1+\gamma a)^2} < 0.
\end{equation}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
Substitute $K_{\mathit{rev}}(a) = K_{\mathit{rev}}(0)(1+\delta a)$ and $V(x,a) = V_0(x)(1+\gamma a)$ into $\eta = K_{\mathit{rev}}(a)/V(x,a)$ and differentiate with respect to $a$ using the quotient rule.
The numerator of the result is $K_{\mathit{rev}}(0)[\delta(1+\gamma a) - \gamma(1+\delta a)] = K_{\mathit{rev}}(0)(\delta - \gamma) < 0$ since $\gamma > \delta$.
\hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Lemma A.2 (Both pass rates rise with $a$).}

\textit{In Cases~II and~III, writing $\dot\eta \equiv \partial\eta/\partial a < 0$:}
\begin{align}
  \frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a} &= -\dot\eta\,(1-\lambda) + \eta\,\mu > 0,
    \label{eq:app_dpi0} \\
  \frac{\partial\pi_1}{\partial a} &= -\dot\eta\,\varepsilon > 0.
    \label{eq:app_dpi1}
\end{align}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}
Differentiate $\pi_0 = 1 - \eta(1-\lambda(a))$ with respect to $a$.
The product rule gives $-\dot\eta(1-\lambda) + \eta\,d\lambda/da = -\dot\eta(1-\lambda) + \eta\mu$.
Both terms are non-negative because $\dot\eta < 0$, $1-\lambda > 0$, $\eta > 0$, and $\mu \geq 0$; the first term is strictly positive.
Differentiate $\pi_1 = 1 - \eta\varepsilon$ with respect to $a$; since $\varepsilon$ is a fixed parameter, $\partial\pi_1/\partial a = -\dot\eta\varepsilon > 0$.
\hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Lemma A.3 (Bad-paper advantage grows with $a$).}

\textit{In Cases~II and~III, (i) the ratio $\pi_0/\pi_1$ is strictly increasing in $a$:}
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d}{da}\!\left(\frac{\pi_0}{\pi_1}\right) > 0;
  \label{eq:app_ratio}
\end{equation}
\textit{and (ii) defining the base pool $M_0 \equiv N\alpha\pi_1 + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0$, the following key inequality holds:}
\begin{equation}
  M_0\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a} - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial M_0}{\partial a}
  = N\alpha\!\left(\pi_1\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a}
     - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial\pi_1}{\partial a}\right) > 0.
  \label{eq:app_key}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textit{Proof of part (i).}
By the quotient rule, the sign of $d(\pi_0/\pi_1)/da$ equals the sign of $\pi_1\,\partial\pi_0/\partial a - \pi_0\,\partial\pi_1/\partial a$.
Substituting \eqref{eq:app_dpi0}--\eqref{eq:app_dpi1}:
\begin{align*}
  &\pi_1\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a} - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial\pi_1}{\partial a} \\
  &\quad= (1-\eta\varepsilon)\bigl[-\dot\eta(1-\lambda)+\eta\mu\bigr]
    - (1-\eta(1-\lambda))\bigl[-\dot\eta\varepsilon\bigr] \\
  &\quad= \dot\eta\Bigl[\varepsilon\bigl(1-\eta(1-\lambda)\bigr)
          - (1-\lambda)\bigl(1-\eta\varepsilon\bigr)\Bigr]
       + \eta\mu(1-\eta\varepsilon).
\end{align*}
Expand the bracket in the first term:
\[
  \varepsilon\bigl(1-\eta(1-\lambda)\bigr) - (1-\lambda)\bigl(1-\eta\varepsilon\bigr)
  = \varepsilon - (1-\lambda)
  = \varepsilon + \lambda - 1 < 0,
\]
where the last inequality uses condition~\eqref{eq:app_filter}.
Since $\dot\eta < 0$ and $(\varepsilon+\lambda-1) < 0$, the first term $\dot\eta(\varepsilon+\lambda-1) > 0$.
The second term $\eta\mu(1-\eta\varepsilon) \geq 0$.
Therefore $\pi_1\,\partial\pi_0/\partial a - \pi_0\,\partial\pi_1/\partial a > 0$ and $d(\pi_0/\pi_1)/da > 0$.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Proof of part (ii).}
Expand $M_0\,\partial\pi_0/\partial a - \pi_0\,\partial M_0/\partial a$ using $M_0 = N\alpha\pi_1 + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0$ and $\partial M_0/\partial a = N\alpha\,\partial\pi_1/\partial a + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\,\partial\pi_0/\partial a$:
\begin{align*}
  &M_0\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a} - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial M_0}{\partial a} \\
  &= \bigl[N\alpha\pi_1 + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0\bigr]\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a}
     - \pi_0\Bigl[N\alpha\frac{\partial\pi_1}{\partial a}
       + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a}\Bigr] \\
  &= N\alpha\!\left(\pi_1\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a}
     - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial\pi_1}{\partial a}\right).
\end{align*}
The $N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0\,\partial\pi_0/\partial a$ terms cancel exactly, leaving $N\alpha(\pi_1\,\partial\pi_0/\partial a - \pi_0\,\partial\pi_1/\partial a) = N\alpha\pi_1^2\,(d/da)(\pi_0/\pi_1) > 0$ by part (i).
\hfill$\square$

\bigskip

\paragraph{Proof of Proposition~1 (Monotone Destabilisation).}

\noindent\textit{Claim: Under $\gamma > \delta \geq 0$, $\mu \geq 0$, $\phi > 0$, $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(x,a)$ is strictly increasing in $a$ for every $x \in (0,1)$.
Consequently, the GH region in $(K_{\mathit{rev}},K)$ space shrinks weakly as $a$ rises, and there exists a finite threshold $a^* \in (0,1]$ above which no $(K_{\mathit{rev}},K)$ pair sustains GH without additional institutional intervention.}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 1: Show $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial a > 0$ in each case.}

\noindent\textbf{Case~I} ($\eta=1$, $\bar\rho=1$):
In the uncongested, unrationed region $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}} = r\lambda(a) - c(a)$, so $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial a = r\mu + c_0\phi > 0$.

\noindent\textbf{Case~II} ($\eta<1$, $\bar\rho=1$):
$\Pi_{\mathit{bad}} = r\pi_0(x,a) - c(a)$, so $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial a = r\,\partial\pi_0/\partial a + c_0\phi > 0$ by Lemma~A.2 and $\phi > 0$.

\noindent\textbf{Case~III} ($\eta<1$, $\bar\rho<1$):
Writing $M_0 \equiv N\alpha\pi_1 + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0$ and $M = (1+\gamma a)M_0$,
we have $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}} = rK\pi_0/M - c(a)$.
Differentiating using $\partial M/\partial a = \gamma M_0 + (1+\gamma a)\,\partial_a M_0$ gives:
\[
  \frac{\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}}{\partial a}
  = \underbrace{\frac{rK(1+\gamma a)}{M^2}\Bigl(M_0\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a}
    - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial M_0}{\partial a}\Bigr)}_{A\;=\;rK(1+\gamma a)N\alpha(\pi_1\partial_a\pi_0-\pi_0\partial_a\pi_1)/M^2\;>\;0
    \;\text{(Lemma~A.3)}}
  \;-\; \underbrace{\frac{rK\gamma\pi_0}{(1+\gamma a)M}}_{B\;>\;0}
  + \underbrace{c_0\phi}_{C\;>\;0}.
\]
The term $B$ is negative, reflecting that the larger submission pool tightens slot rationing.
The sign of $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial a$ equals the sign of $A - B + C$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Sufficient condition.}
Since $B = r\gamma\bar\rho\pi_0/(1+\gamma a)$ and $\bar\rho < 1$, we have $B < r\gamma\pi_0/(1+\gamma a) \leq r\gamma$.
Using $A = rK(1+\gamma a)N\alpha(\pi_1\partial_a\pi_0 - \pi_0\partial_a\pi_1)/M^2$ and Lemma~A.3, a sufficient condition for $A + C > B$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:caseIII_suff}
  N\alpha(1+\gamma a)\bigl[|\dot\eta|(1-\lambda-\varepsilon)+\eta\mu\pi_1\bigr]
  \;>\; \gamma\pi_0 M_0,
\end{equation}
which holds when $\gamma$ is moderate relative to the screening signal $1-\lambda-\varepsilon$
(i.e., when submission volume does not grow so fast that slot-queue congestion overwhelms the screening-degradation and cost-reduction forces).

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Numerical confirmation.}
For both calibrations in Table~\ref{tab:calibration}, condition~\eqref{eq:caseIII_suff} is satisfied and direct evaluation confirms $A - B + C > 0$ throughout $(x,a)\in(0,1)\times[0,1]$, so $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial a>0$ in Case~III.

\noindent\textbf{Step 2: Implication for the GH region.}
The GH regime requires $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(1,a) < 0$ (a single deviating author cannot profitably spam).
Since $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(1,a)$ is strictly increasing in $a$ (Step~1 evaluated at $x=1$), any $(K_{\mathit{rev}},K)$ satisfying GH at $a=0$ may violate it at higher $a$.
The GH region therefore shrinks weakly as $a$ increases.

\noindent\textbf{Step 3: Existence of a finite threshold $a^*$.}
At $x=1$ in Case~I (the least restrictive case, providing a conservative bound), $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(1,a) = r(\lambda_0+\mu a) - c_0(1-\phi a)$.
Setting this to zero and solving:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:app_astar}
  a^* = \frac{c_0 - r\lambda_0}{r\mu + c_0\phi}.
\end{equation}
The denominator is strictly positive since $\phi > 0$, $r, c_0 > 0$, $\mu \geq 0$.
The numerator is positive under the baseline GH condition $c_0 > r\lambda_0$ (spamming is unprofitable without congestion at $a=0$).
Hence $a^* \in (0,\infty)$.
When congestion is already active at $x=1$ (Cases~II or~III), $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(1,a)$ rises faster than \eqref{eq:app_astar} implies, so the formula provides an upper bound.
\hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proof of Proposition~2 (Slot Expansion Paradox).}

\noindent\textit{Claim: Holding $K_{\mathit{rev}}$ and $a$ fixed, increasing $K$ weakly raises $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(x,a)$ at every $x$.
The marginal destabilising effect $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial K$ is strictly larger at higher $a$.}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 1: Sign of $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial K$.}
When $M(x,a) \leq K$, rationing is inactive ($\bar\rho=1$) and $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}$ does not depend on $K$, so $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial K = 0$.
When $M(x,a) > K$, rationing binds ($\bar\rho = K/M$):
\[
  \Pi_{\mathit{bad}} = \frac{rK\,\pi_0}{M} - c, \qquad
  \frac{\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}}{\partial K} = \frac{r\,\pi_0}{M} > 0.
\]
In both cases $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial K \geq 0$, with strict inequality whenever rationing binds.

\noindent\textbf{Step 2: Amplification by $a$.}
When rationing binds, the marginal destabilising effect is $r\pi_0/M$ where $M=(1+\gamma a)M_0$.
Differentiating with respect to $a$ using $M_0 \equiv N\alpha\pi_1 + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0$:
\[
  \frac{\partial^2\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}}{\partial K\,\partial a}
  = \frac{r}{(1+\gamma a)^2 M_0^2}\underbrace{\Bigl[(1+\gamma a)\Bigl(M_0\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial a}
    - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial M_0}{\partial a}\Bigr) - \gamma\pi_0 M_0\Bigr]}_{\text{positive for calibrations in Sets A and B; see \eqref{eq:app_key}}} > 0.
\]
As $a$ rises, $\pi_0$ increases (more bad papers pass the weakened filter) and $c(a)$ falls (the cost of attempting is lower), so each additional publication slot generates a larger increase in the spam payoff.
\hfill$\square$

\bigskip

\paragraph{Proof of Proposition~3 (Erosion of Congestion Defence).}

\noindent\textit{Claim: The slot constraint required to hold $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(0,a) < 0$ is strictly tighter at higher $a$.}

\medskip
When all authors play AS ($x=0$) and rationing is active, the corrupt-boundary stability condition $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}(0,a) < 0$ holds if and only if:
\[
  K < K^*(a) \;\equiv\; \frac{c(a)\,M(0,a)}{r\,\pi_0(0,a)}.
\]
With $M(0,a) = (1+\gamma a)\,N\bigl[\alpha\,\pi_1(0,a) + (1-\alpha)\,\pi_0(0,a)\bigr]$ this becomes:
\[
  K^*(a) = \frac{c(a)\,(1+\gamma a)\,N}{r}\!\left[\alpha\,\frac{\pi_1(0,a)}{\pi_0(0,a)} + (1-\alpha)\right].
\]
We show $K^*(a)$ is strictly decreasing in $a$.
Differentiating with respect to $a$:
\[
  \frac{dK^*}{da}
  = \frac{N}{r}\!\left[
      \underbrace{c_0[\gamma-\phi-2\phi\gamma a]}_{\text{Term 1}}\!\left(\alpha\,\frac{\pi_1}{\pi_0}+(1-\alpha)\right)
      + c(a)(1+\gamma a)\,\alpha\;\underbrace{\frac{d}{da}\!\left(\frac{\pi_1}{\pi_0}\right)}_{\substack{<\;0\;\text{(Lemma A.3)}\\\text{Term 2}}}
    \right].
\]
Term~2 is unambiguously negative.
Term~1's sign depends on the bracket $c_0[\gamma-\phi-2\phi\gamma a]$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Case $\phi \geq \gamma$ (analytical).}
When $\phi \geq \gamma$, the bracket $\gamma-\phi-2\phi\gamma a \leq \gamma-\phi \leq 0$ for all $a\geq 0$,
so Term~1 $\leq 0$.
Both terms are non-positive and Term~2 is strictly negative, giving $dK^*/da < 0$ for all $a\in[0,1]$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Case $\gamma > \phi$ (partial analytical, partial numerical).}
The critical value at which Term~1 changes sign is $a_0 = (\gamma-\phi)/(2\phi\gamma)$.
For $a \geq a_0$, the bracket is non-positive, both terms are non-positive, and $dK^*/da \leq 0$ analytically.
For $a \in [0, a_0)$, Term~1 is positive and may offset part of Term~2.

Under Sets~A and~B ($\gamma=0.5$, $\phi=0.4$), $a_0 = (0.5-0.4)/(2\times0.4\times0.5) = 0.25$.
For $a \geq 0.25$, the result is analytical.
For $a \in [0,0.25)$, direct evaluation confirms Term~2 dominates:
for Set~A, $K^*(0)\approx 268$, $K^*(0.25)\approx 268$, $K^*(0.5)\approx 262$, $K^*(1)\approx 233$,
a monotone decline of about 13\%.
Therefore $K^*(a)$ is strictly decreasing throughout $a\in[0,1]$ under both calibrations.
Therefore, as $a$ rises, the maximum admissible slot count maintaining the corrupt-boundary deterrent falls strictly.
A journal that set $K < K^*(0)$ at baseline will find $K > K^*(a)$ at higher $a$ without changing any institutional parameter; active reduction of $K$ is required to preserve the deterrent.
\hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Proof of Proposition~4 (Policy Lever Ranking).}

\noindent\textit{Claim: Among the four primary levers, reducing $\lambda$ and raising $K_{\mathit{rev}}$ are unconditionally beneficial; raising $c$ is beneficial but imposes symmetric costs on honest authors; expanding $K$ is the unique lever that strictly worsens stability.}

\medskip
We evaluate $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial\theta$ for each lever $\theta \in \{\lambda,\,K_{\mathit{rev}},\,c,\,K\}$.
We work in Case~III (both constraints bind); Cases~I and~II are noted where they differ.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Lever 1: False-positive rate $\lambda\downarrow$.}

Since $\pi_0 = 1-\eta(1-\lambda)$, we have $\partial\pi_0/\partial\lambda = \eta$.
Since $\pi_1 = 1-\eta\varepsilon$ does not involve $\lambda$, $\partial\pi_1/\partial\lambda = 0$, and $\partial M/\partial\lambda = N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\,\eta$.
In Case~III, compute the derivative of $\bar\rho\,\pi_0 = K\pi_0/M$:
\[
  M\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial\lambda} - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial M}{\partial\lambda}
  = M\eta - \pi_0\,N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\,\eta
  = \eta\bigl[M - N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0\bigr]
  = \eta\cdot N\alpha\pi_1 > 0.
\]
Hence $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial\lambda = rK\eta N\alpha\pi_1/M^2 > 0$: reducing $\lambda$ strictly lowers the spam payoff.
There is no adverse side effect on honest authors: the honest-author payoff is $\Pi_{\mathit{good}} = r\bar\rho\,\pi_1 - c$, and $\partial\pi_1/\partial\lambda = 0$; the slight decrease in $M$ (fewer papers pass review when $\lambda$ falls) eases rationing and weakly raises $\bar\rho$, leaving $\Pi_{\mathit{good}}$ unharmed.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Lever 2: Review capacity $K_{\mathit{rev}}\uparrow$.}

When $\eta < 1$, raising $K_{\mathit{rev}}$ increases the screening intensity: $\partial\eta/\partial K_{\mathit{rev}} = 1/V > 0$.
Both pass rates fall:
\[
  \frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial K_{\mathit{rev}}} = -\frac{1-\lambda}{V} < 0,
  \qquad
  \frac{\partial\pi_1}{\partial K_{\mathit{rev}}} = -\frac{\varepsilon}{V} < 0.
\]
In Case~III, compute $M\,\partial\pi_0/\partial K_{\mathit{rev}} - \pi_0\,\partial M/\partial K_{\mathit{rev}}$.
Using $\partial M/\partial K_{\mathit{rev}} = -N[\alpha\varepsilon + (1-\alpha)(1-x)(1-\lambda)]/V$:
\begin{align*}
  &M\,\frac{\partial\pi_0}{\partial K_{\mathit{rev}}} - \pi_0\,\frac{\partial M}{\partial K_{\mathit{rev}}} \\
  &= -\frac{M(1-\lambda)}{V}
     + \frac{\pi_0\,N\bigl[\alpha\varepsilon+(1-\alpha)(1-x)(1-\lambda)\bigr]}{V} \\
  &= \frac{N\alpha}{V}\bigl[\varepsilon\pi_0 - (1-\lambda)\pi_1\bigr].
\end{align*}
Substituting $\pi_0 = 1-\eta(1-\lambda)$ and $\pi_1 = 1-\eta\varepsilon$ and collecting terms:
\[
  \varepsilon\pi_0 - (1-\lambda)\pi_1
  = \varepsilon(1-\eta(1-\lambda)) - (1-\lambda)(1-\eta\varepsilon)
  = \varepsilon + \lambda - 1 < 0,
\]
by condition~\eqref{eq:app_filter}.
Hence $M\,\partial\pi_0/\partial K_{\mathit{rev}} - \pi_0\,\partial M/\partial K_{\mathit{rev}} < 0$, so $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial K_{\mathit{rev}} < 0$: raising review capacity strictly lowers the spam payoff.
In Case~II the same sign follows directly from $\partial\pi_0/\partial K_{\mathit{rev}} < 0$ with $\bar\rho=1$.
In Case~I ($\eta=1$), there is no congestion to relieve and the lever has no effect.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Lever 3: Submission cost $c\uparrow$.}

$\Pi_{\mathit{bad}} = (\cdots) - c$, so $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial c = -1 < 0$ in all cases: raising $c$ strictly lowers the spam payoff.
However, $\Pi_{\mathit{good}} = r\bar\rho\,\pi_1 - c$ satisfies $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{good}}/\partial c = -1 < 0$ as well.
Honest authors bear the same marginal cost increase, and a sufficiently large increase in $c$ eventually drives $\Pi_{\mathit{good}}$ below zero, deterring honest participation.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Lever 4: Slot capacity $K\uparrow$.}

From Proposition~2, $\partial\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}/\partial K \geq 0$, strictly positive whenever rationing is active.
Expanding publication slots raises the expected payoff of submitting low-quality work without any compensating reduction in the pass rate or cost.
Reducing $K$ provides deterrence against spam, but simultaneously lowers $\Pi_{\mathit{good}} = r\cdot\min(1,K/M)\cdot\pi_1 - c$, potentially pushing the system toward No Submission if $K$ is tightened too far.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Summary.}
Reducing $\lambda$ and raising $K_{\mathit{rev}}$ each lower $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}$ strictly and have no direct adverse effect on honest-author payoffs.
Raising $c$ is effective but imposes symmetric costs on all authors.
Expanding $K$ is the unique lever that strictly raises $\Pi_{\mathit{bad}}$: it is the only intervention that unconditionally worsens equilibrium stability.
\hfill$\square$

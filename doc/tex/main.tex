\begin{abstract}
Generative AI is transforming the nature of academic publishing.
By September 2024, more than one-fifth of computer-science preprints showed evidence of large-language-model modification, while monthly submissions to arXiv reached a record 24{,}226, roughly 50\% higher than in late 2022.
Verification has not kept pace: reviewer acceptance rates have declined steadily over two decades, and between 6.5\% and 16.9\% of conference peer reviews are now themselves AI-modified.
We study this asymmetry---the fact that AI scales paper production faster than it scales screening---using a game-theoretic model in which AI adoption raises submission volume and lowers drafting cost faster than it raises review capacity, while also increasing the rate at which low-quality manuscripts pass screening.
We find three results.
First, extreme slot scarcity can stabilize honest submission norms even under degraded screening, because the publication lottery becomes too hard to win.
Second, gradual AI adoption can trigger a discrete tipping point into a low-quality long-run outcome; once tipped, the system is self-sustaining, so recovery requires a large coordinated intervention, not incremental reform.
Third, expanding publication capacity without scaling verification accelerates the tipping.
These results point to a concrete policy agenda of verification-centric institutions (structured audits, submission quotas, and decoupled certification) rather than simply more publication slots.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

We ask what happens to a publishing \emph{system} when it becomes substantially cheaper and faster to produce a manuscript than to review one.
By September 2024, more than one-fifth of computer-science preprints on arXiv showed measurable evidence of large-language-model modification \citep{Liang2025}, and at least 13.5\% of all biomedical abstracts published that year contained AI-assisted text \citep{Kobak2025}.
Monthly submissions to arXiv reached a record 24{,}226 in October 2024, roughly 50\% higher than in late 2022 \citep{arXiv2024}.
Verification has not kept pace.
Reviewer acceptance rates have declined steadily over two decades \citep{Meyerson2025}, and between 6.5\% and 16.9\% of conference peer reviews now contain substantial AI-generated text \citep{Liang2024ICML}.
More than half of active researchers surveyed in 2025 reported using AI tools to write reviews, often against explicit journal policy \citep{Naddaf2025}.
The question is not whether individual researchers behave ethically, but whether the system as a whole can absorb an asymmetric shock to production and verification.

While the conventional response to AI in science focuses on disclosure requirements, detection tools, and editorial guidelines, these measures address individual conduct and leave untouched a structural problem: even if every author is honest, reviewers can scrutinize only a finite number of manuscripts carefully per cycle.
When submission volume rises faster than review capacity, screening degrades---not because of misconduct, but because of congestion.
A reviewer who faces twice as many papers in the same time cannot give each submission the same attention.
Congested screening lowers the effective quality threshold, and a lower threshold raises the expected payoff from submitting weak or low-quality work.
We focus on this feedback between volume and screening quality as the central mechanism.

We distinguish two bottlenecks in the publishing system, and we argue that AI affects them asymmetrically.
The first is verification capacity: the number of papers that reviewers can evaluate carefully in a given period, denoted $K_{rev}$.
The second is allocation capacity: the number of papers the journal can publish per period, denoted $K$.
AI raises submission volume $V$ by reducing the marginal cost of drafting and resubmitting papers, lowering the effort cost $c$ of each submission.
It may also increase the false-positive rate $\lambda$---the probability that a low-quality manuscript passes peer review---because AI-assisted writing produces polished text that is harder to distinguish from genuine contributions on surface features alone.
Review capacity, by contrast, does not scale symmetrically.
Deep expertise, data and code verification, and replication checking remain human-intensive tasks, and journal policies in many fields explicitly restrict AI use in reviews \citep{Naddaf2025}.
Production, in short, scales faster with AI adoption than verification does.

We model this asymmetry in a game-theoretic framework, drawing on strategic models of scientific communication \citep{Zollman2024, Thurner2011}.
We consider a population of authors who choose between two strategies: submitting only genuine work (the cooperative strategy) or submitting everything including low-quality manuscripts (the defecting strategy).
A journal applies peer review, subject to both a review capacity $K_{rev}$ and a publication slot constraint $K$.
We introduce an AI adoption intensity parameter $a \in [0,1]$.
Higher $a$ raises submission volume $V$ with elasticity $\gamma$, raises review capacity $K_{rev}$ with elasticity $\delta < \gamma$, lowers submission cost $c$, and raises the false-positive rate $\lambda$.
The production elasticity exceeds the verification elasticity by assumption, formalizing the asymmetry observed empirically.
The net effect of $a$ on the profitability of submitting a low-quality paper, $\Pi_{bad}(x, a) = r \cdot \bar{\rho}(x,a) \cdot \pi_0(x,a) - c(a)$, is positive: AI makes spamming more attractive.

We find three results.
First, extreme publication scarcity stabilizes honest submission norms even when screening is imperfect.
When the publication lottery is rare enough to win, the expected payoff from submitting a low-quality paper falls below the submission cost, and defection is unprofitable.
Scarcity, paradoxically, acts as a deterrent.
Second, as $a$ rises, the system can cross a tipping threshold: the long-run outcome shifts abruptly from a high-quality equilibrium to a low-quality one.
This tipping occurs because AI simultaneously lowers the break-even point for spam and erodes the screening mechanism that would otherwise deter it.
Third, the system exhibits persistent path dependence.
The value of $a$ at which quality collapses is strictly lower than the value at which quality recovers.
Once the system has tipped into the low-quality long-run outcome, incremental reforms cannot restore it; a large coordinated intervention is needed to cross the recovery threshold.

While expanding publication capacity (raising $K$) seems like the obvious remedy, we find that doing so without raising review capacity makes the situation worse, not better.
More slots increase the expected reward from submitting marginal work, accelerating the drift toward the defecting strategy.
Effective intervention must instead target the two primary levers: raising genuine review capacity $K_{rev}$ and reducing the false-positive rate $\lambda$.
Practical instruments that move these levers include structured code and data audit requirements, submission quotas or tokens, cascading triage systems, and layered certification schemes that decouple preprint dissemination from editorial endorsement.
These are not incremental reforms; they constitute a different institutional design, one oriented toward verification rather than volume.

We proceed as follows: Section~\ref{sec:model} presents the game-theoretic model; Section~\ref{sec:dynamics} derives the replicator dynamics and characterizes the equilibrium regimes; Section~\ref{sec:analysis} examines parameter sensitivity, hysteresis, and strategic levers; and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Stylized Facts: AI as an Asymmetric Shock}
\label{sec:stylized_facts}

\paragraph{We draw on evidence that AI dramatically lowers the cost of producing a manuscript.}
Large language models have cut the time required to draft, revise, and resubmit academic text by a documented margin.
In a randomised experiment with 453 college-educated professionals, \citet{NoyZhang2023} found that access to ChatGPT reduced the time to complete an incentivised writing task by approximately 40\% while simultaneously raising assessed output quality by 18 percentage points, with the largest gains accruing to weaker writers.
\citet{StokelWalker2023} report that researchers can revise a full manuscript in under five minutes at an API cost below \$0.50.
These micro-level reductions compound at the submission level: iterative resubmission across journals was once a significant labour cost, and AI converts that friction into a near-zero marginal activity, giving every author---regardless of the underlying quality of their work---a cost-effective reason to submit or resubmit.
In our model, this channel corresponds to a downward shift in the submission cost parameter $c(a)$.

\paragraph{Aggregate data confirm a sharp rise in submission pressure.}
Macro-level data are consistent with the micro-level cost story.
Monthly submissions to arXiv reached a record 24{,}226 in October 2024, roughly 50\% above the late-2022 baseline; the first month to cross 20{,}000 submissions was May 2023, shortly after the public release of ChatGPT \citep{arXiv2024}.
More than one-fifth of computer-science preprints sampled in September 2024 showed measurable evidence of large-language-model modification \citep{Liang2025}, and a vocabulary-based analysis of 15 million biomedical abstracts found that at least 13.5\% of all 2024 \textit{PubMed} papers contain AI-assisted text, with rates exceeding 40\% in some sub-disciplines \citep{Kobak2025}.
Alongside this volume growth, annual retraction counts exceeded 10{,}000 for the first time in 2023, with paper-mill activity implicated in mass retraction events at major publishers.
These data show that submission volume $V$ has shifted materially upward as AI adoption has spread, independently of any change in the fraction of authors who genuinely produce high-quality work.
In our model, this corresponds to an upward shift in $V(x, a)$ with elasticity $\gamma$ with respect to $a$.

\paragraph{While text production scales with AI, verification does not.}
Rigorous peer review requires reading carefully for logical consistency, checking data against claimed analyses, running or inspecting code, and evaluating genuine novelty---tasks that remain human-intensive in ways that text generation is not.
Confidentiality requirements for submitted manuscripts prevent reviewers from uploading papers to third-party AI services, and many journals explicitly forbid AI use in the review process \citep{Naddaf2025}.
Even where AI-assisted reviewing is nominally permitted, the marginal reviewer who can detect a subtle methodological flaw is a scarce specialist whose attention is not fungible with a language model's output.
Reviewer acceptance rates have declined continuously for two decades \citep{Meyerson2025}, and the existing burden is already extreme: reviewers contribute more than 100 million hours annually to the peer review system worldwide, with the load concentrated among a small fraction of active researchers \citep{Kovanis2016}.
We therefore expect that a system already under strain before the AI era cannot absorb a near-doubling of submission volume through a commensurate increase in review capacity.
In our model, this channel is captured by $K_{rev}(a) = K_{rev}(0) \cdot (1 + \delta a)$, where the elasticity $\delta$ is strictly smaller than the production elasticity $\gamma$, reflecting the structural and policy constraints described above.

\paragraph{AI also polishes manuscripts without improving their reliability, which may raise the false-positive rate.}
A third channel operates through the quality of the screening signal rather than through volume.
AI writing tools produce fluent, well-formatted, and stylistically consistent text \citep{StokelWalker2023}, and this surface polish reduces the ability of reviewers to distinguish a low-quality submission from a genuine contribution on textual features alone, raising the probability that a flawed manuscript passes the review filter.
The screening process degrades further when reviewers themselves turn to AI: between 6.5\% and 16.9\% of AI-conference peer reviews now contain substantial AI-generated text \citep{Liang2024ICML}, and an experimental audit found that AI-assisted reviews award systematically higher scores, leading to elevated acceptance rates \citep{Latona2024}.
Automated detection offers limited recourse---leading AI detectors exhibit false-negative rates above 70\%, and adversarial paraphrasing can reduce recall to near zero \citep{Liang2023detectors}.
In our model, this channel corresponds to an upward shift in the false-positive rate $\lambda(a)$: as AI adoption rises, the probability that a low-quality submission passes review increases even holding the underlying composition of the author population constant.

\paragraph{We summarize these channels as a single asymmetry hypothesis.}
The four channels described above---rising submission volume, falling drafting cost, lagging review capacity, and rising false-positive risk---reduce to a single structural claim:
\begin{quote}
  \textit{AI raises the elasticity of manuscript production above the elasticity of manuscript verification.}
\end{quote}
We formalise this as the \textbf{AI asymmetry assumption}: with $a \in [0,1]$ denoting AI adoption intensity,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:asymmetry}
    \gamma > \delta \geq 0,
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ is the elasticity of submission volume with respect to $a$ and $\delta$ is the elasticity of review capacity with respect to $a$.
This is not an axiom but a testable empirical statement about relative scaling rates.
Table~\ref{tab:ai_channels} translates each channel into a specific model parameter and states the direction of the shift.
In Section~\ref{sec:analysis}, we examine how our qualitative results depend on the magnitude of the gap $\gamma - \delta$ and on the slope of $\lambda(a)$.

\section{Related Literature}
\label{sec:literature}

We draw on research showing that peer review functions as a shared resource under increasing strain.
Each submission consumes a fixed amount of reviewer time and attention, and when submission volume grows faster than the pool of willing reviewers, that resource degrades.
\citet{gross2019contest} show that scientific contests become inefficient at high participation rates: total effort rises, but expected quality per participant falls.
\citet{Cotton2013} reaches a parallel conclusion for journals: a small entry cost can deter low-quality submissions and relieve pressure on reviewers.
\citet{Kovanis2016} estimate that the global peer review system already consumed over 100 million hours of researcher time each year in the mid-2010s, and \citet{Aczel2021} value the US contribution alone at more than 1.5 billion USD in 2020.
\citet{Bianchi2018} model these pressures and find that screening quality declines even when reviewers act in good faith, simply because competing demands leave too little time per paper.
The reviewing system, in short, was already near its limits before generative artificial intelligence entered the picture.

While congestion models treat reviewers as passive, a related body of work models authors as strategic agents---and this is where the feedback loop becomes visible.
\citet{Thurner2011} show that when the review filter is imprecise, submitting everything regardless of quality can become individually rational, pushing the long-run outcome toward low average quality.
\citet{Zollman2024} extend this to a multi-journal setting and find that prestige-maximising incentives can reward strategic over honest behaviour.
\citet{Tiokhin2021} study honest signalling and show that the good outcome---where only strong work is submitted---is fragile: it collapses when the cost of mimicking quality falls even slightly.
\citet{Smaldino2016} document this logic empirically, arguing that ``publish or perish'' incentives act as a selection force that spreads high-volume, low-quality strategies through academic communities.
At the aggregate level, \citet{Ioannidis2005} shows that most published findings fail to replicate, not because of fraud, but because structural incentives reward output over reproducibility.
\citet{casadevall2014reforming} and \citet{Stephan2012} trace this to career and grant structures that treat paper counts as a proxy for scientific value.
Paper mills---commercial services producing fabricated manuscripts to order---represent the extreme end of this logic: annual retraction counts have reached record highs and single incidents have implicated thousands of papers at major publishers.
Our model formalises the mechanism underlying these trends: when the review filter is weak and slots are available, submitting low-quality work yields a positive expected payoff, and selection pressure then favours volume over quality.

In contrast to the strategic submission literature, recent empirical work documents how generative artificial intelligence reshapes both sides of the system simultaneously.
On the production side, \citet{NoyZhang2023} find that access to a large language model cuts professional writing time by 40\% and raises rated output quality by 18 percentage points.
\citet{Liang2025} detect language-model modification in more than one-fifth of computer-science preprints by late 2024, and \citet{Kobak2025} find AI-assisted text in at least 13.5\% of biomedical abstracts from the same period.
On the evaluation side, however, the picture is more troubling.
\citet{Checco2021} conclude that deep verification---checking data, code, and logical consistency---remains beyond current automated systems.
\citet{Liang2024ICML} find that between 6.5\% and 16.9\% of reviews at major conferences already contain AI-generated text, and \citet{Latona2024} show experimentally that AI-assisted reviews award higher scores, raising acceptance rates.
\citet{Naddaf2025} reports that more than half of surveyed researchers now use AI tools for reviewing, often against journal policy.
These findings supply the empirical foundation for our asymmetry assumption: artificial intelligence raises submission productivity faster than it raises verification capacity.

A fourth strand addresses the economics of gatekeeping and institutional design.
\citet{Ellison2002} shows that the equilibrium publication standard depends on how strongly readers value quality signals.
\citet{MullerItten2022} finds that journals maintain strict standards to protect their certification value: accepting too freely devalues their own signal.
\citet{casadevall2014reforming} argue that effective reform must target the false-positive rate directly---through data deposition, structured reporting, and replication requirements---rather than adjusting slot counts alone.
\citet{Ginsparg2011} shows that dissemination and certification can be separated: preprint servers make manuscripts available immediately while editorial endorsement follows independently.
\citet{Cotton2013} provides the sharpest economic case for submission fees: a small cost of entry restores friction, filters marginal submissions, and reduces reviewer overload.

\subsection*{What we contribute}

While these literatures each address part of the problem, none unifies them.
Models of strategic submission do not treat artificial intelligence as a structural shock that shifts multiple inputs at once.
The empirical AI-in-science literature documents faster production, slower review growth, and higher false-positive risk, but does not connect these to a model with clear predictions about the long-run state of the system.
Neither strand addresses the possibility that submission pressure can grow gradually until it crosses a threshold, after which the system shifts suddenly to a low-quality state and resists recovery.
The institutional design literature does not ask how its proposals must change when drafting cost approaches zero and the false-positive rate rises continuously with adoption.
We close these gaps by connecting all four channels---$c(a)$ falling, $V$ rising, $K_{rev}$ lagging, and $\lambda(a)$ rising---to the long-run state of the publishing system, characterising conditions under which quality collapses suddenly and is hard to restore, and deriving which institutional levers can prevent or reverse that shift.

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}
\subsection{Setting}

We consider a publication game involving a finite population of $N$ authors and a single academic journal. 
Our framework aligns with established game-theoretic models of scientific publishing \cite{Thurner2011}, while specifically building on the incentive structures analyzed by Zollman et al. \cite{Zollman2024}.
Unlike previous static models, we extend the analysis to account for science volume.
The fundamental unit of the game is a research paper, which possesses a binary intrinsic quality $q \in \{0, 1\}$. 
A paper is defined as ``good'' ($q=1$) or ``bad'' ($q=0$). 
In each period, authors make submission decisions, and the journal enforces quality control standards subject to a physical capacity constraint.

The publication process unfolds in a strictly sequential timeline, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:process_flow}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Submission (Generation of $V$):} 
    Based on their strategies, authors decide whether to submit their work. 
    The aggregate collection of all submitted manuscripts forms the \textit{Submission Pool}, denoted by $V \le N$.
    
    \item \textbf{Peer Review (Generation of $M$):} 
    The journal applies a peer review process to filter the submission pool. 
    Acknowledging the inherent unreliability of assessment documented in empirical studies \cite{Bornmann2011}, we model reviewers as imperfect classifiers.
    They are subject to Type I errors (False Positives, rate $\lambda$) and Type II errors (False Negatives, rate $\epsilon$). 
    Papers that successfully pass this screening form the set of \textit{Peer-accepted papers}, denoted by $M$. 
    It is important to note that membership in $M$ signifies that a paper is ``scientifically acceptable,'' but not necessarily ``published.''
    
    \item \textbf{Capacity Check and Storage ($K$):} 
    The journal has a fixed resource limit or \textit{Journal Storage}, denoted by $K$. 
    This constraint reflects the physical limits on publication slots or the administrative burden of processing manuscripts \cite{Kovanis2016}.
    
    \item \textbf{Final Acceptance ($\rho$):} 
    The final publication decision depends on the relationship between the volume of peer-accepted papers ($M$) and the journal storage ($K$). 
    We define $\rho$ as the \textit{probability of being accepted conditional on being peer-accepted}. 
    This variable captures the congestion effect:
    \begin{equation}
        \rho = \begin{cases} 
            1 & \text{if } M \le K \quad \text{(No Congestion)} \\
            K/M & \text{if } M > K \quad \text{(Rationing triggered)} 
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

In this framework, a paper is published if and only if it passes two hurdles: the quality hurdle (peer review) and the congestion hurdle (storage constraint).

\subsection{Players, strategies, and quality generation}\label{subsec:players_strategies}

The publication game involves a single journal ($J$) and a finite population of authors $\mathcal{A}=\{1,2,\dots,N\}$.  
We treat $N$ as a finite exogenous parameter. 
This finite-population approach allows us to capture the congestion dynamics and ``tragedy of the commons'' effects inherent in resource-constrained peer review systems, as analyzed in the economic literature on academic publishing \citep{Cotton2013, Azar2005}.
Throughout, we use $V$ and $M$ to denote the realised \emph{counts} of submitted and peer-accepted papers, respectively.

\paragraph{Quality generation.}
In each round, each author generates a manuscript with binary intrinsic quality $q\in\{0,1\}$, where $q=1$ denotes a ``good'' paper and $q=0$ a ``bad'' paper. 
Quality is generated i.i.d.\ across authors with
\begin{equation}
    \Pr(q=1)=\alpha,\qquad \Pr(q=0)=1-\alpha,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha\in(0,1)$ is an exogenous productivity parameter reflecting the baseline scientific capability of the community \citep{Ellison2002}.

\paragraph{Author strategies ($S_A$).}
Each author chooses a submission rule $s_A\in S_A$ that conditions on realised quality. 
Consistent with signaling models in scientific communication \citep{Tiokhin2021}, we consider four pure strategies:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Only Good (OG):} submit iff $q=1$;
    \item \textbf{Always Submit (AS):} submit regardless of $q$ (strategic spamming);
    \item \textbf{Only Bad (OB):} submit iff $q=0$ (included for completeness);
    \item \textbf{Never Submit (NS):} never submit.
\end{itemize}
Equivalently, $s_A$ can be represented by $(p_g,p_b)$, the submission probabilities for good and bad papers:
\begin{equation}
(p_g,p_b)=
\begin{cases}
(1,0) & \text{if } s_A=\text{OG}\\
(1,1) & \text{if } s_A=\text{AS}\\
(0,1) & \text{if } s_A=\text{OB}\\
(0,0) & \text{if } s_A=\text{NS}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Journal strategies ($S_J$).}
The journal commits to an editorial policy $s_J\in S_J$ that maps the perceived quality signal $\hat q\in\{0,1\}$ into an accept/reject decision:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Only Good (OG):} accept iff $\hat q=1$;
    \item \textbf{Always Accept (AA):} accept all submissions (ignores $\hat q$);
    \item \textbf{Only Bad (OB):} accept iff $\hat q=0$;
    \item \textbf{Reject All (AR):} reject all submissions (ignores $\hat q$).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Exogenous parameters and endogenous variables.}
To clarify the mechanics of congestion, we distinguish between fixed environmental constraints and emergent outcomes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Exogenous parameters:} Population size ($N$), productivity ($\alpha$), journal size ($K$), review noise parameters ($\epsilon,\lambda$), and payoff primitives, discussed below: $c$, cost of submission for authors; $r$, the reward for an author derived from successful publication; $B$, the benefit to the journal derived from publishing good papers; and $D$, the the penalty for journals from publishing bad papers.
    \item \textbf{Endogenous variables:} Submission volume ($V$), peer-accepted volume ($M$), the rationing factor ($\rho = \max \left\{ \frac{K}{M}, 1 \right\} $), and the population state $x$ (strategy shares) governing the equilibrium distribution.
\end{itemize}\label{subsec:environment}

\subsection{Review noise and acceptance probabilities}
\label{subsec:review_noise}

Peer review acts as a stochastic filter between the submission volume $V$ and the peer-accepted volume $M$. 
Throughout, \emph{pass} refers to passing the review filter and entering the peer-accepted pool $M$ (not final publication, which is also affected by the journal size.

\paragraph{Noise parameters ($\epsilon,\lambda$).}
Consistent with empirical meta-analyses on review reliability \citep{Bornmann2010, Cicchetti1991}, we assume reviewers are imperfect classifiers. 
Let $\hat q\in\{0,1\}$ denote the perceived quality signal for a paper with true quality $q\in\{0,1\}$.
We define:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon=\Pr(\hat q=0\mid q=1),\qquad
    \lambda=\Pr(\hat q=1\mid q=0),
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon$ is the false negative rate (acting as a tax on honesty) and $\lambda$ the false positive rate (acting as an incentive for opportunism), a structure supported by empirical studies on scientific gatekeeping \citep{Siler2015}.

\paragraph{Conditional acceptance probabilities.}
Given a journal policy $s_J$, define the probability of acceptance conditional on true quality as:
\begin{equation}
    \pi_q(s_J) \equiv \Pr(\text{pass}\mid q, s_J),\qquad q\in\{0,1\}.
\end{equation}
Under the journal strategies defined in Section~\ref{subsec:players_strategies}, and aligned with theoretical models of gatekeeper selection \citep{MullerItten2022}, the acceptance vector $(\pi_1, \pi_0)$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
    (\pi_1(s_J),\pi_0(s_J))=
    \begin{cases}
    (1-\epsilon,\ \lambda) & \text{if } s_J=\text{OG}\\
    (1,\ 1) & \text{if } s_J=\text{AA}\\
    (\epsilon,\ 1-\lambda) & \text{if } s_J=\text{OB}\\
    (0,\ 0) & \text{if } s_J=\text{AR}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Per-author pass-review probability ($\mathcal{P}$).}
Let $(p_g(s_A),p_b(s_A))$ denote the submission probabilities conditional on realised quality. 
We define $\mathcal{P}(s_A,s_J)$ as the \emph{ex-ante} probability that an author playing $s_A$ contributes a paper to the peer-accepted pool $M$ under journal policy $s_J$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{P}(s_A,s_J)=\alpha\,p_g(s_A)\,\pi_1(s_J) + (1-\alpha)\,p_b(s_A)\,\pi_0(s_J).
\end{equation}

Below we enumerate the effective acceptance probabilities for all author strategies under each journal regime.

\textbf{Case 1: Standard Review (Journal plays Only Good).}
This is the baseline scenario where $\pi_1=1-\epsilon$ and $\pi_0=\lambda$.
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{P}(\text{OG},\text{OG}) &= \alpha(1-\epsilon),\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{AS},\text{OG}) &= \alpha(1-\epsilon) + (1-\alpha)\lambda,\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{OB},\text{OG}) &= (1-\alpha)\lambda,\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{NS},\text{OG}) &= 0.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

\textbf{Case 2: Permissive (Journal plays Always Accept).}
The screening is ignored ($\pi_1=1, \pi_0=1$), making acceptance depend solely on submission.
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{P}(\text{OG},\text{AA}) &= \alpha,\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{AS},\text{AA}) &= \alpha + (1-\alpha) = 1,\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{OB},\text{AA}) &= 1-\alpha,\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{NS},\text{AA}) &= 0.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

\textbf{Case 3: Inverse Selection (Journal plays Only Bad).}
The journal accepts papers perceived as bad ($\hat{q}=0$).
Good papers pass only via false negatives ($\pi_1=\epsilon$), and bad papers pass via true negatives ($\pi_0=1-\lambda$).
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{P}(\text{OG},\text{OB}) &= \alpha\epsilon,\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{AS},\text{OB}) &= \alpha\epsilon + (1-\alpha)(1-\lambda),\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{OB},\text{OB}) &= (1-\alpha)(1-\lambda),\\
    \mathcal{P}(\text{NS},\text{OB}) &= 0.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

\textbf{Case 4: Closed (Journal plays Reject All).}
No papers enter the peer-accepted pool ($\pi_1=0, \pi_0=0$).
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{P}(s_A, \text{AR}) = 0 \quad \forall s_A \in S_A.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Aggregate peer-accepted volume ($E[M]$).}
To determine the system-wide congestion, we aggregate these individual passing probabilities across the entire population, a method consistent with burden estimation in peer review \citep{Kovanis2016}.
Let $\mathbf{x} = \{x_{s_A} \mid s_A \in S_A\}$ denote the \textbf{population state}, where $x_{s_A} \in [0, 1]$ represents the proportion of authors adopting strategy $s_A$, satisfying $\sum_{s_A \in S_A} x_{s_A} = 1$.

The expected total volume of peer-accepted papers, $E[M]$, is the sum of contributions from all strategy groups:
\begin{equation}
    E[M] = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathcal{P}(s_{A,i}, s_J) = N \sum_{s_A \in S_A} x_{s_A} \, \mathcal{P}(s_A, s_J).
\end{equation}
This aggregate volume $E[M]$ is the critical endogenous variable that interacts with the journal capacity $K$ to determine the rationing probability $\rho$ defined in Eq.~(1).

\subsection{Capacity constraint and rationing probability ($\rho$)}
\label{subsec:rationing}

A key feature of the model is the separation between \emph{scientific merit} (passing peer review and entering the set of peer-accepted papers) and \emph{publication slots} (capacity-constrained publication).
This distinction captures the reality that top-tier journals often reject scientifically sound papers solely due to space limitations \citep{Ellison2002, casadevall2014reforming}.

\paragraph{Journal capacity ($K$).}
Let $K\in\mathbb{N}^+$ denote the journal's exogenous publication capacity: at most $K$ papers can be published per cycle due to limited pages and editorial resources \citep{Kovanis2016, Azar2005}.

\paragraph{Uniform rationing and the rationing probability ($\rho$).}
Let $M$ be the realised number of peer-accepted papers in a given round. 
If $M \le K$, all peer-accepted papers are published. 
If $M > K$, the journal applies \emph{uniform rationing}: it selects exactly $K$ papers uniformly at random from the $M$ peer-accepted papers. 
Hence, the conditional publication probability for a peer-accepted paper is determined by the realised congestion:
\begin{equation}
    \rho(M)\equiv \Pr(\text{published}\mid \text{pass})=
    \min\!\left(1,\frac{K}{M}\right)=
    \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if } M \le K,\\
    \frac{K}{M} & \text{if } M > K.
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:rho_definition}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Ex-post vs.\ ex-ante congestion.}
Across rounds, the peer-accepted count $M$ is stochastic because it aggregates individual submission and review outcomes. 
Authors therefore form expectations over congestion given the population state $\mathbf{x}$ and journal policy $s_J$, implying an expected rationing factor:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\rho]=\mathbb{E}\!\left[\min\!\left(1,\frac{K}{M}\right)\right].
\end{equation}
Crucially, increases in low-quality or opportunistic submissions raise the expected volume $\mathbb{E}[M]$ and mechanically reduce $\mathbb{E}[\rho]$, generating a negative congestion externality on all authors.
This mirrors the inefficiencies found in scientific funding competitions, where excessive participation degrades the system's overall value \citep{Cotton2013, gross2019contest}.

\subsection{Payoff analysis}
\label{subsec:payoffs}

In this subsection, we derive the expected payoffs for both authors and the journal.
The introduction of the capacity constraint $K$ fundamentally couples the payoffs: an individual's utility depends not only on their own strategy but also on the aggregate congestion generated by the population.
This structure formally aligns with the class of \emph{congestion games}, where individual payoffs are negatively correlated with the total utilization of a shared resource \citep{Rosenthal1973}.

\paragraph{Notation and general framework}

Table~\ref{tab:variables_updated} summarizes the key parameters and variables.
We employ a \emph{mean-field approximation} where players base their decisions on the \textbf{expected} state of the system.
This approach is standard in evolutionary game theory for modeling interactions in large finite populations, allowing deterministic analysis of stochastic processes \citep{Sandholm2010, Weibull1995}.

\paragraph{Submission and publication probabilities.}
For an author playing strategy $s_A \in S_A$, we define the unconditional probability of submitting a paper as $P_{\text{sub}}(s_A)$.
Based on the submission vectors $(p_g, p_b)$ defined in Section~\ref{subsec:players_strategies}, this is:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{sub}}(s_A) = \alpha p_g(s_A) + (1-\alpha) p_b(s_A).
\end{equation}
Recall from Section~\ref{subsec:review_noise} that $\mathcal{P}(s_A, s_J)$ denotes the probability of \emph{entering the peer-accepted pool} $M$.
However, entering $M$ does not guarantee publication.
Let $\bar{\rho}$ denote the \textbf{expected rationing probability}:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\rho} \equiv \mathbb{E}[\rho] = \mathbb{E}\left[ \min\left(1, \frac{K}{M} \right) \right].
\end{equation}
The joint probability of a paper being finally published is the product of passing review and passing the capacity check: $\bar{\rho} \cdot \mathcal{P}(s_A, s_J)$.

\paragraph{Volumes: Submitted ($V$), Passed ($M$), and Published ($Y$).}
It is crucial to distinguish between papers that pass peer review ($M$) and those that are finally published ($Y$).
Let $V_G$ and $V_B$ be the expected volumes of good and bad submissions generated by the population.
Let $M_G$ and $M_B$ be the expected volumes of papers entering the peer-accepted pool.
We define $Y_G$ and $Y_B$ as the expected volumes of \textbf{finally published} papers:
\begin{equation}
    Y_G = \bar{\rho} M_G, \quad Y_B = \bar{\rho} M_B.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{General payoff functions}

\paragraph{Author's utility ($U_A$).}
Let $r$ be the reward for a published paper and $c$ be the cost of submission.
The expected payoff for an author playing $s_A$ against a journal $s_J$ is the expected reward from publication minus the reliable cost of submission:
\begin{equation}
    U_A(s_A, s_J) = r \cdot \underbrace{\bar{\rho} \cdot \mathcal{P}(s_A, s_J)}_{\text{Prob(Published)}} - c \cdot \underbrace{P_{\text{sub}}(s_A)}_{\text{Prob(Submitted)}}
    \label{eq:author_payoff_general}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Journal's utility ($U_J$).}
Let $B$ be the benefit of publishing a good paper, $D$ be the penalty for a bad paper, and $C_J$ be the unit cost of reviewing a submission.
The journal's utility depends on the \emph{published} volumes ($Y_G, Y_B$) and the \emph{submitted} volume ($V = V_G + V_B$).
This formulation captures the trade-off between quality assurance and processing costs \citep{MullerItten2022}:
\begin{equation}
    U_J(s_J) = B \cdot Y_G - D \cdot Y_B - C_J \cdot V
\end{equation}
Substituting $Y = \bar{\rho} M$, we obtain the working form of the journal's payoff:
\begin{equation}
    U_J(s_J) = \bar{\rho} \left[ B \cdot M_G(s_J) - D \cdot M_B(s_J) \right] - C_J \cdot V
    \label{eq:journal_payoff_general}
\end{equation}
\textit{Remark:} Eq.~(\ref{eq:journal_payoff_general}) relies on the approximation $\mathbb{E}[\rho M] \approx \mathbb{E}[\rho]\mathbb{E}[M]$.
As $N$ grows large, the relative fluctuations in $M$ diminish, making this mean-field limit exact \citep{Sandholm2010}.

\paragraph{Derivation of strategy-specific payoffs}

We now explicitly list the payoff functions for the 8 primary scenarios (4 Author strategies and 4 Journal strategies).

\paragraph{Author payoffs (under Journal Strategy $s_J = \text{OG}$).}
In the standard academic context, the journal plays \textbf{Only Good (OG)}.
The review filter applies $(\pi_1, \pi_0) = (1-\epsilon, \lambda)$.
Substituting the specific $\mathcal{P}$ and $P_{\text{sub}}$ values into Eq.~(\ref{eq:author_payoff_general}), we derive:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:payoff_author_OG}
    U_A(\text{OG}, \text{OG}) &= \alpha \left[ r \bar{\rho} (1-\epsilon) - c \right] \\
    \label{eq:payoff_author_AS}
    U_A(\text{AS}, \text{OG}) &= r \bar{\rho} \left[ \alpha(1-\epsilon) + (1-\alpha)\lambda \right] - c \\
    \label{eq:payoff_author_OB}
    U_A(\text{OB}, \text{OG}) &= (1-\alpha) \left[ r \bar{\rho} \lambda - c \right] \\
    \label{eq:payoff_author_NS}
    U_A(\text{NS}, \text{OG}) &= 0
\end{align}

\paragraph{Journal payoffs (under Population State $\mathbf{x}$).}
The journal chooses a strategy $s_J$ to maximize utility given the incoming flow of submissions.
Let $V_G(\mathbf{x})$ and $V_B(\mathbf{x})$ be the volumes of good and bad submissions determined by the population state.

\textbf{1. Only Good (OG):}
The journal filters papers.
The passed volumes are $M_G = V_G(1-\epsilon)$ and $M_B = V_B\lambda$.
\begin{equation}
    U_J(\text{OG}) = \bar{\rho} \left[ B V_G(1-\epsilon) - D V_B\lambda \right] - C_J(V_G + V_B)
\end{equation}

\textbf{2. Always Accept (AA):}
The journal accepts everything ($M_G = V_G, M_B = V_B$).
Congestion is maximized here, so $\bar{\rho}$ will be minimal.
\begin{equation}
    U_J(\text{AA}) = \bar{\rho} \left[ B V_G - D V_B \right] - C_J(V_G + V_B)
\end{equation}

\textbf{3. Only Bad (OB):}
The journal applies inverse selection ($M_G = V_G \epsilon, M_B = V_B(1-\lambda)$).
\begin{equation}
    U_J(\text{OB}) = \bar{\rho} \left[ B V_G \epsilon - D V_B(1-\lambda) \right] - C_J(V_G + V_B)
\end{equation}

\textbf{4. Reject All (AR):}
The journal publishes nothing ($Y=0$).
Review costs are still incurred for processed submissions.
\begin{equation}
    U_J(\text{AR}) = - C_J(V_G + V_B) \quad (\text{normalized to } 0 \text{ in dominance analysis})
\end{equation}

\subsection{Dominance analysis and reduced game}
\label{subsec:dominance}

Before analyzing the evolutionary dynamics, we simplify the strategy space by iteratively eliminating strictly dominated strategies.
This procedure, known as \emph{iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies} (IESDS), allows us to focus on the rationalizable core of the game \citep{Fudenberg1991}.
We conduct this analysis under the standard \textbf{mean-field approximation}, holding the aggregate congestion factor $\bar{\rho}$ fixed.
The elimination proceeds in two stages: first, we identify strategies that are dominated purely by the structural logic of the game (independent of specific cost-benefit inequalities); second, we introduce parameter constraints to eliminate strategies based on economic trade-offs.

\paragraph{Stage 1: Structural elimination (Unconditional dominance)}

We first establish dominance relations that hold under weak axiomatic assumptions regarding the nature of peer review and scientific value, consistent with signaling models in scientific communication \citep{Tiokhin2021}.

\paragraph{Elimination of Journal "Only Bad" (OB).}
The strategy \textbf{Only Bad (OB)} represents an inverse selection policy.
We compare it to \textbf{Only Good (OG)}.
Using the payoff functions derived in Section~\ref{subsec:payoffs}, the difference in expected utility is:
\begin{align}
    \Delta U_J &= U_J(\text{OG}) - U_J(\text{OB}) \nonumber \\
    &= \bar{\rho} \left[ B V_G (1-\epsilon) - D V_B \lambda \right] - \bar{\rho} \left[ B V_G \epsilon - D V_B (1-\lambda) \right] \nonumber \\
    &= \bar{\rho} \left[ B V_G (1 - 2\epsilon) + D V_B (1 - 2\lambda) \right]
\end{align}
\textbf{Condition:} We assume peer review is \textbf{informative}, defined as performing better than random guessing:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon < 0.5 \quad \text{and} \quad \lambda < 0.5.
\end{equation}
Under this definition, both $(1-2\epsilon)$ and $(1-2\lambda)$ are strictly positive. 
Since $B, D, V_G, \bar{\rho} > 0$, it follows that $\Delta U_J > 0$.
Thus, \textbf{Journal OB is strictly dominated by OG} solely by virtue of the definition of peer review quality, regardless of the magnitude of rewards or penalties.

\paragraph{Elimination of Author "Only Bad" (OB).}
Consider the choice between \textbf{Always Submit (AS)} and \textbf{Only Bad (OB)}.
AS submits both paper types ($q \in \{0,1\}$), while OB submits only bad papers ($q=0$).
The payoff difference is the marginal utility of submitting a good paper:
\begin{align}
    \Delta U_A &= U_A(\text{AS}, \text{OG}) - U_A(\text{OB}, \text{OG}) \nonumber \\
    &= \left[ U_A(q=1) + U_A(q=0) \right] - U_A(q=0) \nonumber \\
    &= U_A(q=1) = \alpha \left[ r \bar{\rho} (1-\epsilon) - c \right]
\end{align}
\textbf{Condition:} We assume the \textbf{Axiom of Value}: in a functional scientific system, a correctly identified high-quality paper yields a positive net return (i.e., $r(1-\epsilon) > c$). 
If this axiom holds, then $\Delta U_A > 0$ for any non-zero congestion $\bar{\rho}$.
Thus, \textbf{Author OB is strictly dominated by AS} due to monotonicity: a rational author will never withhold a high-quality paper while simultaneously submitting a low-quality one.

\paragraph{Parameter constraints for a non-trivial game}

Having eliminated the structurally irrational strategies, we now focus on the remaining set.
To ensure the game yields interesting evolutionary dynamics (avoiding trivial collapse or utopian perfection), we define the "viable region" of the parameter space using the \textbf{abundance benchmark} ($\bar{\rho}=1$).

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Viability Constraint (Honesty is profitable):}
    \begin{equation}
        r(1-\epsilon) > c
        \label{eq:constraint_viability}
    \end{equation}
    This formally restates the Axiom of Value.
    It ensures that without congestion, the honest strategy (OG) dominates silence (NS), a necessary condition for any scientific activity \citep{gross2019contest}.

    \item \textbf{Profitability Constraint (Spamming is profitable):}
    \begin{equation}
        \lambda r > c
        \label{eq:constraint_profitability}
    \end{equation}
    This constraint drives the congestion dilemma.
    It implies that in a frictionless system ($\bar{\rho}=1$), the expected reward of a bad paper exceeds the submission cost.
    If $c > \lambda r$, bad papers are never submitted, and the game trivially converges to perfect honesty.

    \item \textbf{Reputation Constraint (Screening is preferred):}
    \begin{equation}
        D V_B (1-\lambda) > B V_G \epsilon
        \label{eq:constraint_reputation}
    \end{equation}
    This condition ensures the journal prefers screening (OG) over blind acceptance (AA). 
    It states that the aggregate penalty avoided by rejecting bad papers outweighs the benefit lost from accidentally rejecting good ones \citep{MullerItten2022}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Stage 2: Conditional elimination (Economic dominance)}

We now eliminate strategies based on the specific economic trade-offs defined above.

\paragraph{Dominance of Journal "Always Accept" (AA).}
We compare \textbf{Only Good (OG)} with \textbf{Always Accept (AA)}. 
Assuming the processing cost $C_J$ applies to all submissions (Processing Cost Model), the difference depends on the net value of papers rejected by OG but accepted by AA:
\begin{align}
    U_J(\text{OG}) - U_J(\text{AA}) &= \bar{\rho} \left[ (B M_G^{\text{OG}} - D M_B^{\text{OG}}) - (B M_G^{\text{AA}} - D M_B^{\text{AA}}) \right] \nonumber \\
    &= \bar{\rho} \left[ D (V_B - M_B^{\text{OG}}) - B (V_G - M_G^{\text{OG}}) \right] \nonumber \\
    &= \bar{\rho} \left[ D V_B (1-\lambda) - B V_G \epsilon \right]
\end{align}
Under the \textbf{Reputation Constraint} (\ref{eq:constraint_reputation}), the penalty avoided (first term) exceeds the benefit lost (second term).
Thus, \textbf{AA is strictly dominated by OG}.
This result aligns with gatekeeper theory, suggesting that competitive journals maintain strict standards to maximize their distinctiveness and reputation \citep{MullerItten2022}.

\paragraph{Dominance of Journal "Reject All" (AR).}
Strategy AR yields a normalized payoff of $0$ (or $-C_J V$). 
Since OG generates positive utility from good papers ($B > 0$) and filters bad ones, OG dominates AR provided the net value of the peer-accepted pool is positive: $B M_G > D M_B$. 
We assume the journal operates in a parameter region where valid science is net positive.

\paragraph{Dominance of Author "Never Submit" (NS).}
Comparing \textbf{Only Good (OG)} with \textbf{Never Submit (NS)} ($U_A=0$):
\begin{equation}
    U_A(\text{OG}, \text{OG}) - U_A(\text{NS}, \text{OG}) = \alpha \left[ r \bar{\rho} (1-\epsilon) - c \right]
\end{equation}
Under the \textbf{Viability Constraint} (\ref{eq:constraint_viability}), this difference is positive as long as the congestion does not exceed the critical threshold $\bar{\rho} > \frac{c}{r(1-\epsilon)}$.
In the viable regime, \textbf{NS is strictly dominated by OG} (and by AS).
This condition reflects the rational participation constraint found in scientific contests, where authors only enter if the expected payoff exceeds the effort cost \citep{gross2019contest}.

\paragraph{The reduced evolutionary game}

Following the two-stage elimination process, the complex multi-strategy interaction reduces to a concise $2 \times 1$ asymmetric game.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Journal} is a fixed institutional player locked into the \textbf{Only Good (OG)} strategy. It acts as the environment, applying a filter $(\epsilon, \lambda)$ and a capacity constraint $K$.
    \item \textbf{The Author Population} evolves between two surviving pure strategies:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Only Good (OG) [Cooperators]:} Authors who submit only high-quality work ($q=1$).
        \item \textbf{Always Submit (AS) [Defectors]:} Authors who submit everything ($q \in \{0,1\}$), effectively spamming the journal with bad papers to exploit $\lambda$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

We define the \textbf{population state} $x(t) \in [0,1]$ as the fraction of authors adopting the \textbf{Only Good} strategy.
The evolutionary dynamics of the system are thus entirely determined by the time evolution of $x$.
This reduction captures the essential dynamic of scientific evolution: the selection pressure between high-effort integrity and low-effort quantity \citep{Smaldino2016}.

\section{Replicator Dynamics and Bifurcation Analysis}
\label{sec:dynamics}

\subsection{Population state and congestion functions}
\label{subsec:endogenous_congestion}

Following the dominance analysis, the strategy space reduces to a contest between two author strategies, with the journal playing a fixed filtering role.
We define the \textbf{population state} $x(t) \in [0, 1]$ as the fraction of authors playing \textbf{Only Good (OG)}, while $1-x(t)$ play \textbf{Always Submit (AS)}.
We assume the system operates in the region $\lambda r < c < r$: in the absence of congestion, submitting low-quality work is unprofitable, but it becomes viable once the screening mechanism degrades sufficiently---a mechanism analogous to the market for lemons \citep{Akerlof1970, Thurner2011}.

To capture the dual impact of congestion, we model two distinct bottlenecks.
Numerical simulations use a smoothed approximation to capture pre-congestion friction; the analytical forms below use standard cutoffs for clarity.

\paragraph{Stage 1: The screening intensity function $\eta(x)$}
The total submission volume is $V(x) = N [ 1 - (1 - \alpha)x ]$.
The screening intensity $\eta(x)$ represents the probability that a paper undergoes rigorous peer review.
It degrades when volume exceeds the review capacity $K_{rev}$, a phenomenon consistent with models of "reviewer fatigue" and resource scarcity \citep{Azar2005, Kovanis2016}:
\begin{equation}
    \eta(x) = \min\left(1, \frac{K_{rev}}{V(x)}\right)
\end{equation}
We assume ``rubber-stamping'' implies unconditional acceptance of unscreened papers.
This reflects the transition from rational selection to random selection observed in overloaded systems \citep{Thurner2011}.
Thus, the pass probability for a bad paper, $\pi_0(x)$, increases as screening fails:
\begin{equation}
    \pi_0(x) = \eta(x) \lambda + (1 - \eta(x)) \cdot 1 = 1 - \eta(x)(1-\lambda)
\end{equation}
For a good paper, the pass probability $\pi_1(x)$ also approaches 1 as scrutiny relaxes (assuming false negatives $\epsilon$ only occur during rigorous review):
\begin{equation}
    \pi_1(x) = \eta(x) (1-\epsilon) + (1 - \eta(x)) \cdot 1 = 1 - \eta(x)\epsilon
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Stage 2: The rationing probability function $\bar{\rho}(x)$}
Let $K$ be the publication slot capacity.
The expected volume of papers entering the ``perceived good'' pool $M(x)$ is:
\begin{align}
    M(x) &= V_G(x)\pi_1(x) + V_B(x)\pi_0(x) \nonumber \\
    &= N\alpha \cdot \pi_1(x) + N(1-\alpha)(1-x) \cdot \pi_0(x)
\end{align}
Using a mean-field approximation, the expected rationing probability is:
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\rho}(x) \approx \min\left(1, \frac{K}{M(x)}\right)
\end{equation}
This formulation captures the ``lottery nature'' of high-impact publishing: when the number of scientifically sound papers ($M$) exceeds the journal's physical space ($K$), acceptance becomes a game of chance rather than pure merit \citep{casadevall2014reforming}.

\subsection{Derivation of the replicator equation}

Since both OG and AS strategies submit good papers whenever they occur (with identical acceptance probabilities), the expected payoff component from good papers cancels out in the differential.
The dynamics are driven entirely by the net utility of the additional "bad" papers submitted by AS authors, denoted as $\Pi_{bad}(x)$.
The replicator equation is:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{x} = -x(1-x)(1-\alpha)\Pi_{bad}(x)
\end{equation}

A bad paper yields reward $r$ only if it passes both bottlenecks.
The expected net utility is:
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_{bad}(x) = r \cdot \bar{\rho}(x) \cdot \pi_0(x) - c
\end{equation}

\subsection{Bifurcation and stability analysis}

The system exhibits \textbf{Bistability} because the two congestion effects work in opposition, creating a dynamic tension characteristic of tipping-point models \citep{Schelling1978}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Review Collapse ($\pi_0 \uparrow$):} Increases the incentive to cheat (pro-corruption).
    \item \textbf{Storage Rationing ($\bar{\rho} \downarrow$):} Decreases the incentive to cheat (pro-honesty).
\end{itemize}
When the review collapse effect dominates at low honesty, $\Pi_{bad}(x)$ becomes positive, creating a \emph{corruption trap} where opportunistic strategies proliferate \citep{Smaldino2016}.
Conversely, when strict screening dominates at high honesty, $\Pi_{bad}(x)$ becomes negative, creating a basin of honesty.

\paragraph{Analytical Condition for Instability of the Internal Equilibrium}

To rigorously characterize the stability of an interior equilibrium $x^* \in (0,1)$, we analyze the local derivative of the velocity field.
Following standard evolutionary stability analysis \citep{Hofbauer1998}, the equilibrium is unstable (a repeller) if and only if the net utility of cheating is locally decreasing in $x$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d \Pi_{bad}}{d x} \bigg|_{x^*} < 0
\end{equation}
Recall that $\Pi_{bad}(x) = r \cdot \bar{\rho}(M(x)) \cdot \pi_0(x) - c$.
Using the chain rule, we decompose the total derivative into two competing forces:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{d \Pi_{bad}}{d x} = r \left( \underbrace{\bar{\rho}(M) \frac{d \pi_0}{d x}}_{\text{Review Effect}} + \underbrace{\pi_0(x) \frac{d \bar{\rho}}{d M} \frac{d M}{d x}}_{\text{Rationing Effect}} \right)
\end{equation}
Note that $\frac{d \pi_0}{d x} < 0$ (since higher honesty reduces volume $V$, improving review quality $\eta$, thus lowering $\pi_0$).
Conversely, the rationing term is typically positive (higher honesty reduces false positives in $M$, relaxing rationing, thus increasing $\bar{\rho}$).
Therefore, the tipping point is unstable if and only if the Review-Dominated Condition holds locally:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:instability_condition}
    \underbrace{\left| \bar{\rho}(M) \frac{d \pi_0}{d x} \right|}_{\text{Loss from stricter review}} > \underbrace{\pi_0(x) \frac{d \bar{\rho}}{d M} \frac{d M}{d x}}_{\text{Gain from relaxed rationing}}
\end{equation}
This condition confirms that bistability arises when the recovery of peer review quality (as $x$ increases) outweighs the relaxation of publication slots.


\subsection{Equilibrium regimes}

\paragraph{Stable coexistence: rationing-dominated dynamics}

While the default scenario exhibits bistability, a fundamentally different topological regime emerges when the storage constraint ($K_{store}$) is significantly tighter than the review constraint ($K_{rev}$).
Figure \ref{fig:stable_coexistence} illustrates this scenario, where the evolutionary velocity $\dot{x}$ transitions from positive to negative, creating a Stable Internal Equilibrium ($x^* \approx 0.44$).

\paragraph{Existence and Stability Conditions}

For a stable coexistence equilibrium to exist, two conditions must be met:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Boundary Conditions:} The system must be repelled from both extremes. This requires $\Pi_{bad}(1) > 0$ (Temptation at Honesty) and $\Pi_{bad}(0) < 0$ (Unprofitability at Corruption). By the Intermediate Value Theorem, there must exist at least one root $x^* \in (0,1)$ where $\Pi_{bad}(x^*) = 0$.
    \item \textbf{Stability Condition:} The slope of the velocity field must be negative, i.e., $\frac{d \Pi_{bad}}{d x} \big|_{x^*} > 0$. This corresponds to the Rationing-Dominated Condition*, where the "punishment" from overcrowding (tightening $\bar{\rho}$) outweighs the "opportunity" from collapsed screening.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Real-world implication: The ``Defense by Congestion'' paradox}

The emergence of a stable internal equilibrium reveals a paradoxical phenomenon: \textbf{extreme congestion acts as an unintentional defense mechanism}. 
In journals with strict page limits ($K_{store} \ll N$), even if peer review fails ($\pi_0 \to 1$), the sheer volume of submissions drives the acceptance rate $\bar{\rho}$ so low that spamming becomes statistically unprofitable. 
This explains why top-tier journals do not degenerate into full corruption even under heavy load—the "lottery" is simply too hard to win.
This dynamic aligns with contest models where excessive competition discourages entry by marginal participants \citep{gross2019contest}.
However, it also implies vulnerability: if capacity is expanded (increasing $K_{store}$), the system loses this passive defense, potentially shifting the topology to bistability or collapse.
This highlights the strategic value of artificial scarcity in maintaining the "gatekeeper" function of academic journals \citep{MullerItten2022}.
\paragraph{Boundary regimes: global honesty and global corruption}

Beyond the interior dynamics, the system may exhibit monotonic behaviour where the incentive to submit low-quality work is uniformly positive or negative.

\paragraph{Global Honesty ($x^* = 1$): Defense by Rigor}

When $\Pi_{bad}(x) < 0$ for all $x \in [0,1]$, the evolutionary velocity $\dot{x}$ remains positive throughout the domain, driving the system unconditionally toward $x^* = 1$ (Figure \ref{fig:global_honesty}).
A sufficient condition for this regime is that the payoff is negative even at the most favorable boundary ($x=1$):
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_{bad}(1) = r \cdot \lambda - c < 0
\end{equation}
This implies that suppressing the false-positive rate $\lambda$ below the cost-reward ratio $c/r$ is the primary safeguard for global honesty.

\section{Parameter Sensitivity and Regime Transitions}
\label{sec:analysis}


\subsection{Methodology and Baseline Calibration}

We want to know which policies maximize publication quality and minimize academic misconduct.
However, due to the non-linear interplay between the review bottleneck ($\eta$) and the storage bottleneck ($\bar{\rho}$), the system does not respond smoothly to policy changes.
Instead, the ecosystem undergoes phase transitions between qualitatively distinct regimes.

To analyze these transitions, we use numerical bifurcation analysis.
We define the instantaneous Average Publication Quality $Q(x)$ as the proportion of good papers within the set of accepted publications, a metric that aligns with standard meta-scientific frameworks for assessing the reliability of research findings \citep{Ioannidis2005}:
\begin{equation}
    Q(x) = \frac{N\alpha \cdot \pi_1(x)}{M(x)} = \frac{\alpha \pi_1(x)}{\alpha \pi_1(x) + (1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0(x)}
\end{equation}
Crucially, the long-term quality $Q_\infty$ is determined by the system's stable attractors.
In bistable regimes, $Q_\infty$ is path-dependent, meaning the final quality depends on whether the system started from a high-trust or low-trust state.

We classify the system into four regimes based on the stability of equilibria:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Global Honesty (GH):} the system converges to $x=1$, yielding $Q_\infty = 1.0$.
    \item \textbf{Global Corruption (GC):} the system collapses to $x=0$, yielding $Q_\infty \approx \alpha$---a ``market for lemons'' where the journal fails to filter noise \citep{Akerlof1970}.
    \item \textbf{Bistability (BS):} two stable attractors coexist and the outcome depends on history.
    \item \textbf{Stable Coexistence (SC):} an interior equilibrium $x^*$ is maintained by congestion, stabilizing quality at a mediocre level ($Q_\infty \approx 0.5$).
\end{enumerate}

To ensure comparative consistency, we use two representative parameter sets (Table \ref{tab:calibration}).
Set~A represents a typical journal with moderate capacity, prone to bistability.
Set~B represents a top-tier journal with strict page limits and high rewards, prone to stable coexistence.

\paragraph{Why two parameter sets?}

A single baseline cannot capture the full range of the system's dynamical behaviours, because the dual-bottleneck mechanism is non-monotonic.
We distinguish two archetypes.
Set~A represents the default state of many modern journals: moderate prestige ($r=100$) and limited but not extreme storage constraints ($K=400$).
Here the primary failure mode is the review bottleneck ($K_{rev}=300$)---when submission volume rises, the review process collapses before the storage limit is reached.
This setup reflects the empirical reality of the ``global burden'' of peer review, where reviewer scarcity is the dominant constraint \citep{Kovanis2016}, and it generates a bistable topology in which the system is fragile and prone to catastrophic tipping.
Set~B represents top-tier prestige journals such as \textit{Nature} or \textit{Science}, where the reward is significantly higher ($r=150$) and the defining constraint is not the lack of reviewers (we assume ample review capacity $K_{rev}=1200$) but the extreme scarcity of publication slots ($K=180$).
This aligns with empirical findings on scientific gatekeeping, where high rejection rates are driven by physical slot limits rather than quality filtering alone \citep{Siler2015, casadevall2014reforming}, and it generates the stable coexistence topology that illustrates what we call the defence-by-congestion mechanism.
By analysing both sets, we decouple the effects of review collapse from slot rationing, ensuring that our conclusions are robust across different segments of the academic publishing hierarchy.

\subsection{The Decoupling of Capacities: Phase Diagram Analysis}

Standard academic models often treat ``journal capacity'' as a single resource.
Our dual-bottleneck framework reveals that the system's fate depends on how two distinct capacities interact: review capacity $K_{rev}$, which determines the screening intensity $\eta(x)$, and slot capacity $K_{store}$, which determines the rationing probability $\bar{\rho}(x)$.
High $K_{rev}$ prevents review collapse; low $K_{store}$ creates what we call a defence by congestion.

To visualize the interaction between these forces, we perform a two-dimensional bifurcation scan across the parameter space $(K_{rev}, K_{store})$.
The resulting phase diagram (Figure \ref{fig:regime_map}) maps the system's regimes.

    \textbf{Green (Global Honesty):} High review capacity ensures cheating is unprofitable ($Q \to 1.0$).
    \textbf{Blue (Stable Coexistence):} Extreme slot scarcity ($K_{store} \ll N$) triggers the "Defense by Congestion," stabilizing the system at a mediocre but functional level.
    \textbf{Yellow (Bistability):} A transition zone where the outcome is history-dependent.
    \textbf{Red (Global Corruption):} When slots are abundant but review is weak, the defense mechanism vanishes, leading to total collapse ($Q \to \alpha$).

The topology of Figure \ref{fig:regime_map} highlights two critical dynamical phenomena:

\paragraph{The paradox of slot expansion.}
Intuition suggests that giving a journal more publication slots should improve the system.
We find the opposite.
Moving vertically from the bottom of the diagram corresponds to increasing $K_{store}$.
Initially, increasing slots rescues the system from total collapse (Gray to Blue), making participation rational for honest authors.
However, further increasing $K_{store}$ pushes the system from stable coexistence (Blue) into global corruption (Red) or bistability (Yellow).
This paradox arises because tight storage constraints act as a passive defence by congestion, keeping $\bar{\rho}$ low enough to deter spam.
By increasing $K_{store}$, the journal inadvertently dismantles this defence: $\bar{\rho}$ rises, the publication lottery becomes winnable again, and if $K_{rev}$ is not increased simultaneously, the incentive to cheat reactivates and the system tips into full corruption.
This finding aligns with strategic gatekeeping models showing that expanding capacity without scaling review rigour destroys the signal value of the journal \citep{MullerItten2022}.

\paragraph{The necessity of review resources.}
Moving horizontally to the right corresponds to investing in review capacity $K_{rev}$.
Provided the system is in a feasible region (not Gray), we find that increasing $K_{rev}$ is unconditionally beneficial, expanding the Global Honesty (Green) zone.
Crucially, the boundary between corruption and honesty slopes upward: as $K_{store}$ increases, the journal requires \textit{disproportionately} more review capacity to maintain honesty.
This implies that larger journals are inherently harder to police than smaller ones---they require a review infrastructure that scales super-linearly with volume to maintain the same level of integrity, exacerbating the already critical burden of peer review \citep{Kovanis2016}.

\subsection{Population Pressure and Hysteresis: The Cost of Lost Trust}

While capacity parameters define the structural limits of the journal, the total population $N$ represents the external load.
One might expect quality to decline smoothly as load increases.
We find instead that the system exhibits hysteresis in the bistable regime: the value of $N$ at which quality collapses is much higher than the value at which it recovers.
This is consistent with theories of catastrophic shifts in complex systems, where state recovery requires significantly more effort than state maintenance \citep{Scheffer2001}.

To capture this path dependence, we use a numerical adiabatic continuation method.
We define the system state $x_{st}(N)$ as the stable equilibrium selected by continuity from the previous state:
\begin{equation}
    x_{st}(N_i) = \arg\min_{x \in \mathcal{E}_{st}(N_i)} |x - x_{st}(N_{i-1})|
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{E}_{st}(N_i)$ is the set of stable fixed points at $N_i$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Forward Path (Crowding):} Increments $N$, initializing from $x_{st}(N_{min}) \approx 1$.
    \item \textbf{Backward Path (Recovery):} Decrements $N$, initializing from $x_{st}(N_{max}) \approx 0$.
\end{itemize}
Figure \ref{fig:hysteresis} illustrates the resulting trajectories using Baseline Set A.

\begin{figure}[hbt!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth]{hysteresis_combined.pdf}

    The shaded region represents the bistable interval $[N_{\downarrow}, N_{\uparrow}]$.
    \textbf{Left Panel (State):} On the Forward Path (Blue), the "Honest Norm" is resilient ($x_{st} \approx 1$) until the collapse threshold $N_{\uparrow} \approx 1800$. Once corrupted (Red Dashed), the system is trapped in the low-honesty attractor ($x_{st} \approx 0$). Recovery requires reducing $N$ below the distinct recovery threshold $N_{\downarrow} \approx 350$.
    \textbf{Right Panel (Quality):} The collapse leads to a discrete jump in quality. In the corrupt state (Red Dashed), quality is dominated by the baseline productivity ($\alpha=0.2$) but fluctuates slightly as review intensity $\eta(N)$ varies. Recovery only occurs when the system crosses $N_{\downarrow}$.}
    \label{fig:hysteresis}
\end{figure}

We highlight three features of the result.

\paragraph{Structural resilience (forward path).}
A journal with a strong reputation demonstrates remarkable resilience.
As seen in the blue trajectory, the system maintains $x_{st} \approx 1$ and perfect quality ($Q=1.0$) even when $N$ far exceeds the nominal capacity, because honest authors self-regulate, keeping the effective load $V(1) = N\alpha$ manageable.
The collapse point $N_{\uparrow} \approx 1800$ is the threshold where the honest equilibrium loses stability: $N_{\uparrow} = \inf \{N : \Pi_{bad}(1; N) \ge 0\}$.

\paragraph{The stickiness of corruption (backward path).}
Once the system settles into the corrupt equilibrium, the sheer volume of spam saturates the review capacity.
Even if $N$ is reduced, the system remains trapped in the low-quality state as long as $\Pi_{bad}(0; N) > 0$.
The recovery point $N_{\downarrow} \approx 350$ corresponds to the threshold where the corrupt equilibrium finally becomes unstable.
This asymmetry mirrors the dynamics of collective reputation, where a group remains locked in a low-quality equilibrium long after external conditions improve, because the burden of proof is too high for individual reformers \citep{Tirole1996}.

Crucially, we define the Average Publication Quality $Q(x)$ as the proportion of good papers in the passed pool $M(x)$:
\begin{equation}
    Q(x) = \frac{N\alpha \pi_1(x)}{N\alpha \pi_1(x) + N(1-\alpha)(1-x)\pi_0(x)}
\end{equation}
Since slot rationing ($\bar{\rho}$) applies uniformly to $M(x)$, it does not alter the ratio of good-to-bad papers.
Consequently, quality remains low throughout the backward path until the critical transition occurs.

\paragraph{Policy implication: basin hopping.}
The disparity between $N_{\uparrow}$ and $N_{\downarrow}$ implies that incremental reforms are structurally insufficient for a corrupted journal.
Within the bistable interval, marginal reductions in submissions do not change the long-run attractor.
Escaping the low-quality basin requires interventions strong enough to move the system across the recovery threshold ($N < N_{\downarrow}$).
This suggests that while soft measures work for maintenance, hard measures (e.g., temporary submission moratoriums) are structural necessities for recovery.

\subsection{Strategic Levers and Regime Transitions}

While capacity parameters define the structural limits, the strategic parameters ($r, c, \lambda, \epsilon, \alpha$) determine the incentives.
We perform a comparative sensitivity analysis across all five dimensions, grouping them into three functional categories: screening, economic incentives, and productivity.

\paragraph{Screening parameters: the false-positive rate versus friction.}

We begin with the two parameters controlling the peer review filter (Figure \ref{fig:strategic_part1}).
Panel~(a) reveals that the false-positive rate $\lambda$ is the most critical parameter in the entire system.
As $\lambda$ increases, the system shifts rapidly from Green (Honesty) to Yellow (Bistability) and finally to Red (Corruption); in the yellow zone, a minor perturbation can trigger a catastrophic tip into the corrupt equilibrium.
This aligns with signalling theory, where the reliability of the signal (low $\lambda$) is paramount for maintaining the separating equilibrium \citep{Tiokhin2021}.

In contrast, Panel~(b) shows that friction $\epsilon$ has negligible structural impact: within the explored range, the regime boundaries remain largely unchanged.
This implies that policies focused solely on reducing administrative friction for good papers yield little improvement in integrity compared to improving detection accuracy.

We next examine the financial and reputational incentives (Figure \ref{fig:strategic_part2}).

As legitimate authors produce more papers, the total volume rises and consumes scarce review capacity; if $K_{rev}$ is fixed, this ``good congestion'' dilutes the screening intensity for everyone, paradoxically making the system more vulnerable to invasion by spammers.

\section{Agent-Based Modeling}
\label{sec:abm}

\subsection{Simulation Design}

The analytical results above assume a large, well-mixed population and deterministic strategy evolution---the standard mean-field approximation \citep{Hofbauer1998}.
Real academic communities are finite and subject to stochastic fluctuations \citep{Nowak2006}.
We therefore implement an agent-based model (ABM) to test whether our main claims survive these complications: that dominated strategies die out naturally, that the predicted long-run outcomes emerge from local interactions under noise, and that the defence-by-congestion mechanism is not an artefact of the mean-field limit \citep{Bonabeau2002}.

We simulate a population of $N=1000$ agents, following the standard protocols of stochastic evolutionary game theory in finite populations \cite{Traulsen2009}.
We initialise the population with a uniform distribution across all four strategies (25\% each), so that the simulation must find the reduced state on its own rather than having it assumed.
The process proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Interaction Phase:} In each time step $t$, agents generate submissions based on their full strategy space ($s_i \in \{\text{OG}, \text{AS}, \text{OB}, \text{NS}\}$). The aggregate volume $V$ and peer-accepted volume $M$ are calculated endogenously, determining the congestion levels $\eta$ and $\rho$.
    \item \textbf{Payoff Realization:} Agents receive payoffs $\Pi_i$ based on the realized publication outcomes. This captures the "lottery" nature of the congestion bottleneck.
    \item \textbf{Strategy Update (Social Learning):} We employ the \textbf{pairwise comparison process} to model strategy evolution. A randomly selected agent $A$ considers mimicking agent $B$'s strategy. The probability of switching $s_A \to s_B$ is given by the Fermi function derived from statistical mechanics \cite{Szabo1998}:
    \begin{equation}
        P(s_A \to s_B) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(-\beta [\Pi_B - \Pi_A])}
    \end{equation}
    where $\beta$ represents the intensity of selection.
    This update rule is the standard method for introducing bounded rationality and decision noise into evolutionary dynamics \cite{Traulsen2009}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Simulation Results}

We simulated both parameter sets from Table \ref{tab:calibration} with a high selection intensity ($\beta=1.0$) to reduce noise.
Figure \ref{fig:abm_results} shows that the agent-based simulations corroborate both the strategy elimination and the macroscopic predictions from Section~3.

In both scenarios, the ``Only Bad'' (OB) and ``Never Submit'' (NS) strategies are rapidly driven to extinction within the first few hundred time steps, directly verifying the iterated elimination of strictly dominated strategies (IESDS) conducted in Section~2.6.
The system autonomously reduces to a binary contest between OG and AS, consistent with the finding that strictly dominated strategies cannot survive under monotonic selection dynamics \cite{Samuelson1997}.

In the review-dominated regime (Scenario~A), the dynamics exhibit sharp bistability (Figure \ref{fig:abm_bistability}).
Because the initial proportion of honest OG authors ($25\%$) falls well below the theoretical unstable tipping point ($x^* \approx 0.82$), the system cannot self-correct.
Once OB and NS vanish, the AS strategy outcompetes the remaining honest authors, driving the system into the absorbing state of full corruption ($x=0$).
This confirms the severe path dependence predicted by the analytical model \cite{Scheffer2001, Schelling1978}.

In the rationing-dominated regime (Scenario~B), we observe a qualitatively different outcome (Figure \ref{fig:abm_stable}).
Despite the initial chaotic interaction of all four strategies, the population rapidly settles to a long-run average of $x^* \approx 0.49$ for OG and AS, while OB and NS remain extinct.
This result aligns precisely with the analytical fixed point ($x^* \approx 0.49$) identified in the phase portrait analysis, confirming that the defence-by-congestion mechanism is a robust emergent property, immune to initial strategy turbulence and finite-size stochasticity.

\subsection{Co-operation ABM}

To rigorously validate the robustness of our theoretical findings and explore the microscopic foundations of the ``Defense by Congestion'' mechanism, we develop a specialized Co-operation Agent-Based Model (ABM) featuring local batch-matching.
While the standard replicator dynamics rely on a mean-field approximation assuming an infinite population and a single monolithic market, real-world academic publishing is inherently fragmented.
Authors typically compete within specific sub-disciplines, specialized journals, or distinct temporal submission windows.
The primary motivation for designing this ABM is to investigate whether the macroscopic stability predicted by the ODEs holds when subjected to the localized uncertainty, heterogeneous payoffs, and stochastic fluctuations characteristic of finite populations.

The core innovation of this extended ABM is the \textit{batch-matching} algorithm.
Instead of evaluating all $N$ agents globally, the population is randomly partitioned into local batches (sub-markets) in each time step.
The journal's review and storage capacities ($K_{rev}$ and $K_{store}$) are scaled proportionally to match these local batches.
This approach significantly improves upon standard global ABMs by introducing critical variance into the localized rationing probability ($\rho$) and enabling stochastic drift.
It captures the granular reality that the publication ``lottery'' is not uniform; different sub-fields experience varying degrees of congestion simultaneously.
This localized competition provides a much more rigorous stress-test for the theoretical equilibria.

The simulation results validate our threshold competition framework across two key dimensions, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:co_abm_composite}:

\textbf{Trajectory Analysis and Robustness:} Figures~\ref{fig:co_abm_composite}(a) and~\ref{fig:co_abm_composite}(b) illustrate the time evolution of strategies under batch matching.
In the rationing-dominated regime (Scenario B), despite chaotic local interactions, both the theoretical ODE and the ABM trajectories converge tightly to the stable internal equilibrium of $x^* \approx 0.49$.
This confirms that the stable coexistence induced by congestion is highly robust against local noise.
Conversely, in the review-dominated regime (Scenario A), the ABM demonstrates sharp bistability, collapsing rapidly to global honesty ($x=1.0$) or global corruption ($x=0.0$) depending on initial conditions.
The ABM closely traces the expected theoretical paths but exhibits micro-oscillations, validating the IESDS reduction while underscoring the severe path-dependence of the bistable regime.

\paragraph{The Journal as an Independent Evolutionary Agent}

\paragraph{From Fixed Environment to Co-Evolutionary Agent}
In previous sections, including the local batch-matching analysis in Section 5.4, the journal was mathematically treated as a fixed institutional player locked into the "Only Good" (OG) strategy.
It acted purely as a static environmental constraint enforcing a peer review filter and a capacity limit.
By elevating the journal to an independent, adaptive agent, we transition the framework from a simple population game into an asymmetric co-evolutionary game.
Journals now possess their own complete strategy space, consisting of Only Good (OG), Always Accept (AA), Only Bad (OB), and Reject All (AR).
They update their behavior based on their own distinct utility function, which must balance the reputation benefits of publishing good papers, the penalties of publishing bad papers, and the administrative costs of peer review.
This structural update captures the "Red Queen" dynamic inherent in academic publishing: if the author population becomes overwhelmingly honest, journals are financially incentivized to drop their costly screening processes and adopt the "Always Accept" (AA) strategy.
However, this relaxation immediately triggers an opportunistic invasion of "Always Submit" (AS) authors, which in turn forces the journals to revert to strict filtering.

\paragraph{The Significance of the 10:1 Local Batch-Matching Mechanism}
Real-world academic publishing is not a monolithic global market, but rather a highly fragmented ecosystem divided into specific sub-disciplines and specialized journals.
To accurately model this micro-level reality, we introduce an extreme 10:1 local batch-matching mechanism, pairing 1000 authors with 100 distinct journal agents.
In this configuration, global capacities ($K_{rev}$ and $K_{store}$) are scaled down proportionally to match the size of these local sub-markets.
The primary significance of this 10:1 ratio is the maximization of localized variance and stochastic drift.
By breaking the assumption of uniform global congestion, a single journal agent shifting to a rubber-stamping (AA) strategy creates a massive, localized payoff spike for the specific batch of 10 authors assigned to it, severely testing the structural resilience of the system.

To simulate this environment, we employ an Agent-Based Model (ABM) where both populations update their strategies via a Fermi pairwise comparison process, introducing bounded rationality and decision noise.

The ABM realization demonstrates that structurally dominated strategies, such as Only Bad (OB) and Reject All (AR), are rapidly driven to extinction.
The remaining strategies enter a dynamic coexistence where the continuous mathematical predictions (ODE) and the finite-population ABM trajectories align closely.

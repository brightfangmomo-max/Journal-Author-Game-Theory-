# Julian Garcia -- Rhetorical Patterns and Argumentation Playbook

> Deep guide on how arguments are constructed at the sentence, paragraph, and section level.
> Use alongside `julian-style-guide.md` and `julian-paper-structure.md`.

---

## The Contrastive Engine

The dominant argumentative move across all papers is **contrast**: setting up one position and then pivoting to complicate, qualify, or overturn it. Master this and you capture the core of the writing.

### Pattern 1: The "While... , ..." Concession

The most frequently used construction. Use it to:
- Frame the gap: "While its hormonal underpinnings have been studied extensively, the behavioural and environmental mechanisms remain poorly understood."
- Position your work against alternatives: "While we fully agree with these ambitions, our present proposal is fundamentally different."
- Acknowledge a limitation before moving on: "While these models are useful in explaining some empirical patterns, they have limitations."

**Template:** "While [acknowledged truth about prior work/context], [the complication, gap, or your different angle]."

### Pattern 2: The "However" Pivot

Used as a sentence opener to mark a turn in the argument. Appears in virtually every section.

- After establishing background: "However, little progress has been made on..."
- After stating one effect: "The option to leave however turns out to increase rather than reduce..."
- After presenting a potential objection: "However, how do we know that these are not relevant?"

**Rule:** "However" should introduce genuine new information or a real pivot, not just a mild elaboration.

### Pattern 3: The Dialectical Two-Sides-Then-Resolution

Especially prominent in theory-heavy papers. The argument presents:
1. **Effect A** (e.g., "leaving undermines punishment within a partnership")
2. **Effect B** (e.g., "leaving creates endogenous assortment")
3. **Resolution** (e.g., "the net effect is positive")

This three-beat structure can organize an entire section or a single paragraph.

### Pattern 4: The Paradox Setup

Used for the paper's most striking finding:
- "Paradoxically, our analysis reveals that more truth-motivated debaters can produce worse collective outcomes."
- "This is paradoxical, especially given that..."
- Build to the paradox; do not lead with it in the abstract unless it is the paper's headline result.

## Claim Strength Ladder

Different types of claims get different levels of directness:

### Level 1: Formal/Empirical Results (Strongest)
- "We find that the net effect of introducing the option to leave on cooperation is positive."
- "Our models demonstrate that age-polyethism can emerge from simple social imitation processes."
- "Our study shows F1 scores that are 2.5--3.5 times better than previous works."

**Language:** "We find that...", "We show that...", "Our models demonstrate that..."

### Level 2: Interpretive Claims (Moderate Hedging)
- "Partner choice therefore seems to be at least as powerful a mechanism for the evolution of cooperation as reciprocity is."
- "This phenotypic assortment... may be a better match with the long-lasting cooperation we observe in humans."
- "This suggests that the role of social learning deserves further study."

**Language:** "seems to be", "may be", "suggests that", "points to"

### Level 3: Speculation and Future Directions (Clear Hedging)
- "Future extensions could explore more complex task taxonomies."
- "The model might also illuminate how social media affects the quality of public discourse."
- "It is conceivable that what we know about the emergence of cooperation can inform the design of agents."

**Language:** "could", "might", "it is conceivable that", "may offer a promising path"

### Level 4: Qualified Self-Assessment
- "We should obviously not attach deeper meaning to this exact number, because it is the result of a somewhat arbitrary choice."
- "This label may not perfectly match what we think of as trust-enhancing behaviour, but the idea is that..."

**Language:** Explicit qualification of your own framing choices. Shows intellectual honesty.

## Paragraph-Level Moves

### The Topic-Evidence-Qualify-Implicate Pattern

The standard paragraph shape:

1. **Topic sentence** (claim or setup): "The standard setup in models for the co-evolution of reciprocity and cooperation assumes that randomly matched individuals are tied to their partner."
2. **Evidence/elaboration** (with citations or examples): Supporting detail, numbers, references.
3. **Qualification** (the turn): "However,..." or "While this..." or "We should not attach deeper meaning to..."
4. **Implication** (connection forward): Why this matters, or how it connects to the next point.

### The "This [noun]..." Backward Link

Start a paragraph with "This" + noun phrase to link back to the previous paragraph:
- "This steady state, or short-run equilibrium, describes..."
- "This assortment suppresses the payoffs of defectors."
- "This fundamental uncertainty about the epistemic value of belief revision makes reluctance to change one's mind a rational response."

This creates seamless flow between paragraphs without heavy-handed transitions.

### The Forward Reference

Signal upcoming content to maintain reader orientation:
- "Below, we will see that this is not the case."
- "We will return to this below."
- "In Section X, we will see that punishing by leaving creates endogenous assortment."

### The Concessive Paragraph Opening

When a paragraph must acknowledge something uncomfortable before moving on:
- "There are many ways in which our model is stylized."
- "There are many possible objections to our approach."

Then follow with the rebuttal or qualification.

## Transition Toolkit

### Between paragraphs within a section:
- **Contrastive:** "However,", "While...,", "In contrast,", "On the other hand,"
- **Additive:** "Moreover,", "Additionally,", "Furthermore,"
- **Consequential:** "Consequently,", "This means that...", "It follows that..."
- **Forward-looking:** "Below, we will see that...", "In the following sections, we will try to identify..."
- **Backward-linking:** "This [noun]...", "This is consistent with..."

### Between sections:
- The last paragraph of a section often previews the next section's topic.
- The first paragraph of a new section connects to the previous section before introducing new material.
- Use explicit roadmapping when the paper has complex structure.

## How to Handle Different Argument Types

### Introducing a New Model

1. State what the model is for (one sentence, plain language).
2. Describe the setup verbally before introducing equations.
3. Introduce equations one at a time with "where" clauses.
4. Give intuition for what the math means in plain language.
5. If an algorithm is involved, present pseudocode with line numbers.

### Comparing With Prior Work

1. State what the prior work showed (fairly, with citation).
2. Identify what it did not address (contrastive framing).
3. Explain how your approach differs.
4. Do NOT strawman the prior work.

### Presenting a Counterintuitive Result

1. Set up what the reader might expect.
2. Present the actual result directly.
3. Explain why the intuition fails.
4. Give the mechanism behind the surprising result.

### Acknowledging Limitations

1. State the limitation directly (one sentence).
2. Explain why it does not undermine the core contribution.
3. Optionally suggest how future work could address it.
4. Keep it brief -- one paragraph in the Discussion is usually enough.

## Key Phrases and Constructions

### For introducing your contribution:
- "In this paper, we allow for..."
- "Here, we argue for..."
- "We advance an alternative possible explanation..."
- "We present a novel modelling framework..."

### For stating results:
- "We find that..."
- "Our models demonstrate that..."
- "Our analysis shows that..."
- "As expected, we observe that..."

### For hedging interpretations:
- "seems to be at least as powerful"
- "may be a better match with"
- "it is worthwhile asking whether"
- "it is worthwhile noting that"
- "we believe that our theoretical approach provides plausible evidence"

### For signaling caution about your own model:
- "We should obviously not attach deeper meaning to this exact number"
- "This label may not perfectly match what we think of"
- "There are many ways in which our model is stylized"
- "Although a direct comparison is not meaningful with prior work, we can qualitatively compare"

### For closing a paper:
- End on a **substantive conceptual note**, not a summary.
- The final sentence should suggest broader meaning or connect to empirical reality.
- Example: "This phenotypic assortment, where unrelated, similarly dependable cooperators end up playing with each other, may be a better match with the long-lasting cooperation we observe in humans, who tend to exert some influence over who they cooperate with, if they can, and who cooperate with genetically unrelated others."

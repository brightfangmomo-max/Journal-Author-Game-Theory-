\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

We consider a population of $N$ authors submitting to a single journal over repeated periods.
Each author generates a manuscript with binary quality $q \in \{0,1\}$: good ($q=1$) with probability $\alpha$, and bad ($q=0$) with probability $1-\alpha$.
Iterated elimination of dominated strategies reduces the strategy space to two options \citep{Fudenberg1991}.
Under \textbf{selective submission} (OG), an author submits only when $q=1$.
Under \textbf{universal submission} (AS), an author submits regardless of quality, exploiting the positive false-positive rate to profit from bad papers.
We write $x \in [0,1]$ for the share of authors playing OG; the remaining $1-x$ play AS.
The journal applies a fixed review policy with noise parameters $(\epsilon,\lambda)$ and enforces two capacity limits described below.

The system has two sequential bottlenecks, each creating a distinct strategic force.
The first is \textbf{review capacity} $K_{rev}$.
Total submission volume is $V(x) = N[1-(1-\alpha)x]$, which rises as the AS share grows.
When $V$ exceeds $K_{rev}$, only a fraction of papers receive careful scrutiny; the rest pass automatically---a ``rubber-stamping'' effect documented in overloaded review systems \citep{Azar2005, Kovanis2016}.
The \textbf{screening intensity} is
\begin{equation}
    \eta(x) = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{K_{rev}}{V(x)}\right).
\end{equation}
The second bottleneck is \textbf{publication capacity} $K$.
Papers that survive review enter a peer-accepted pool $M(x)$; at most $K$ can be published per period.
The probability that a peer-accepted paper is ultimately published---the \textbf{rationing probability}---is
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\rho}(x) = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{K}{M(x)}\right).
\end{equation}

Peer review is an imperfect classifier \citep{Bornmann2010, Cicchetti1991}.
A good paper is falsely rejected with probability $\epsilon$ (false negative); a bad paper wrongly passes with probability $\lambda$ (false positive).
Both error rates apply only during rigorous review.
When $\eta < 1$, unscreened papers pass automatically, so the effective pass rate for a bad paper rises above $\lambda$:
\begin{equation}
    \pi_0(x) = 1 - \eta(x)(1-\lambda).
\end{equation}
For a good paper the pass rate is $\pi_1(x) = 1 - \eta(x)\epsilon$, which likewise rises under congestion.
As $\eta \to 0$, both rates converge to 1: the journal loses all discriminatory power.

Because OG and AS authors submit their good papers on identical terms, the strategic difference reduces to the marginal value of submitting a bad paper.
The net payoff from doing so is
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_{bad}(x) = r \cdot \bar{\rho}(x) \cdot \pi_0(x) - c,
    \label{eq:pibad}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the publication reward and $c$ the submission cost.
When $\Pi_{bad}(x) > 0$, universal submission earns more and its share grows; when $\Pi_{bad}(x) < 0$, it shrinks.
We work in the region $\lambda r < c < r(1-\epsilon)$: without congestion, submitting a bad paper loses money, but the incentive reactivates once review degrades---the same adverse-selection logic as in Akerlof's market for lemons \citep{Akerlof1970}.

Strategy shares evolve according to the replicator equation \citep{Weibull1995, Hofbauer1998}:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{x} = -x(1-x)(1-\alpha)\,\Pi_{bad}(x).
    \label{eq:replicator}
\end{equation}
Rest points occur at $x=0$, $x=1$, and any interior root of $\Pi_{bad}(x^*)=0$.
The long-run outcome depends on the signs of $\Pi_{bad}$ at the two boundaries, yielding four qualitatively distinct regimes summarised in Table~\ref{tab:regime_classification}.

Two opposing forces govern the dynamics.
A rise in AS adoption floods the review queue, lowering $\eta$ and raising $\pi_0$: submitting bad papers becomes more attractive.
At the same time it floods the publication queue, lowering $\bar{\rho}$: submitting bad papers becomes less attractive.
When the first force dominates, the system tips toward full corruption.
When the second force dominates, tight slot scarcity deters spam even after review has collapsed---a mechanism we call \emph{defence by congestion}.
Figure~\ref{fig:replicator_dynamics} illustrates the bistable case: $\Pi_{bad}$ is positive near $x=0$ (corruption is self-reinforcing) and negative near $x=1$ (honesty is self-reinforcing), with an unstable tipping threshold separating the two regions.
